% ---------- Title ----------
\title{Point Cloud Retrieval with PQ-Chamfer Distance and Graph Regularization: Training-Free Geometric Operators with a Supervised Per-Query Fusion-Weight Predictor for Weak-Baseline LLM Embeddings}

% ---------- Author ----------
\author{Yifan Chen}
\address{Independent Researcher, China}
\ead{contact-via-github@github.com}

% ---------- Abstract ----------
\begin{abstract}
General-purpose large language model (LLM) hidden states, when used directly for retrieval via mean-pooled centroid cosine, give baselines weaker than retrieval-trained dense encoders. We ask whether \emph{geometric post-processing}---training-free distance and smoothing operators composed with a supervised per-query fusion-weight predictor---can close part of this gap on weak-baseline LLM embeddings.

We introduce a two-stage geometric reranking pipeline on token-level point clouds extracted from a frozen Qwen3-8B-Q4. Stage~1, \textbf{PQ-Chamfer distance}, exploits the product-quantization decomposition $\mathbb{R}^{4096} = \bigoplus_{s=1}^{64} \mathbb{R}^{64}$, computes exact per-subspace cosine distances, and averages them, breaking concentration of measure without quantization error. Stage~2, \textbf{graph Laplacian smoothing} on a $k$-NN document graph built from PQ-Chamfer distances, propagates relevance across inter-document structure. The per-query fusion weight is learned by a supervised gradient-boosted regressor (GBM-11) under a 50/50 train/test split.

On five non-Quora BEIR corpora (NFCorpus, ArguAna, SciFact, SCIDOCS, FiQA), the pipeline lifts the Qwen3-8B token-centroid cosine baseline by $+20.9\%$ to $+157.4\%$ NDCG@10. Paired bootstrap against the per-corpus oracle reference gives $p<0.001$ on four corpora and $p<0.005$ on SciFact (Cohen's $d$ ranges 0.20--0.72). Against retrieval-trained baselines on identical splits in our own infrastructure (audited rebuild after a vector-provenance fix), the pipeline is competitive on weak-baseline corpora but does not displace cross-encoders on short scientific abstracts. This positions the contribution as \emph{geometric post-processing on weak-baseline LLM embeddings}, a regime-specific complement. This work additionally documents 21 falsified approaches (11 with complete raw experimental logs preserved from the development of this research program; 10 retrospectively reconstructed from contemporaneous notes with provenance flagged per-entry) organized into a three-class failure-mechanism taxonomy. The full pipeline cost \$47 on a single consumer GPU.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
% ============================================================

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) has become the dominant paradigm for grounding large language models in external knowledge \citep{lewis2020rag,guu2020realm}. The standard RAG pipeline employs a bi-encoder to map queries and documents into a shared embedding space, then ranks candidates by cosine similarity. Let $\mathbf{v}_q \in \R^d$ and $\mathbf{v}_i \in \R^d$ denote the query and document embeddings, respectively:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cosine}
\text{score}(d_i) = \frac{\mathbf{v}_q \cdot \mathbf{v}_i}{\|\mathbf{v}_q\| \, \|\mathbf{v}_i\|}
\end{equation}

This formulation has two fundamental limitations. First, it represents each document as a \textit{single point} in $\R^d$, conflating diverse semantic facets into one centroid. Second, it scores each document independently, ignoring inter-document structure---topic clusters, semantic neighborhoods, and distributional patterns---that the retrieved set collectively encodes.

Existing training-free remedies do not address these two limitations directly. Query-rewriting methods such as HyDE~\citep{gao2023hyde} and Query2Doc~\citep{wang2023query2doc} alter the query but leave the document representation intact, so cosine concentration in $\R^d$ persists at scoring time. Sparse--dense fusion with BM25~\citep{robertson2009bm25} adds lexical signal but does not recover sub-document structure, and shares cosine's pointwise scoring assumption on the dense side. Pseudo-relevance feedback (PRF) over dense embeddings~\citep{li2023ance-prf} expands the query in embedding space, again inheriting concentration of measure. Late-interaction architectures such as ColBERT and ColBERTv2~\citep{khattab2020colbert,santhanam2022colbertv2} explicitly preserve token-level structure but require dedicated training of a token-aware encoder, contradicting the operator-level training-free regime we target. The gap we identify, therefore, is the absence of a method that (a)~exploits the token-level geometry of frozen general-purpose embeddings and (b)~scores documents jointly through inter-document structure---without any retrieval-specific training.

This gap is addressed by treating documents as point clouds rather than points \citep{chen2026a}. The per-token hidden states of a language model form a cloud in embedding space whose spread, density variation, and subspace projections carry semantic information that a single centroid discards. Two design questions follow: what distance metric is appropriate between document point clouds, and how can inter-document structure be incorporated without the cost of cross-encoder inference? Both are answered with training-free geometric operations: PQ-Chamfer distance for the first, graph Laplacian smoothing for the second.

\textbf{Relation to prior versions.} This paper extends the Shape-CFD research program \citep{chen2026a,chen2026b}. Earlier iterations of this research program explored convection-diffusion equations and sentence-level point clouds. The present work abandons the convection-diffusion narrative---experimentally falsified across five independent validations (Section~\ref{subsec:falsification})---and reorganizes the framework around token-level point clouds, PQ-Chamfer distance, and graph Laplacian smoothing.

\subsection{Contributions}
\label{subsec:contributions}

This work positions itself as a \emph{geometric post-processing layer} along a \textbf{baseline-strength gradient}: across the spectrum from weak frozen-LLM cosine baselines (Qwen3-8B-Q4 token-mean centroid, NDCG@10 $\sim 0.17$--$0.45$ across 5 BEIR corpora), through retrieval-trained dense baselines (BGE-M3, BGE-large-en-v1.5, NDCG@10 $\sim 0.31$--$0.64$), to retrieval-trained late-interaction baselines (ColBERTv2 MaxSim, NDCG@10 $\sim 0.31$--$0.61$), the magnitude of $\Delta$NDCG@10 from geometric post-processing varies non-monotonically with both baseline regime and graph topology quality (e.g., on the same-encoder Qwen3-8B-Q4 main results in Table~\ref{tab:main}, ArguAna's argumentative-text query/document pairs yield rich $k$-NN graph topology and the largest $|\Delta|$ in absolute NDCG@10 terms despite a mid-range cosine baseline, while SCIDOCS' sparse short cite-prediction text yields noisier graph topology and a smaller absolute $|\Delta|$ than its weak baseline alone would predict; per-corpus magnitudes depend jointly on baseline-regime headroom and corpus-level graph-topology signal-to-noise), while the sign of $\Delta$ depends on the baseline regime (uniformly positive on weak baselines; mixed on strong baselines, with the third-tier ColBERTv2 + Lap result reported in Section~\ref{subsec:colbertv2-lap}). This baseline-strength gradient---rather than a universal ``always-positive'' claim---is the central evidence pattern of our work, and it positions the geometric post-processor as a \emph{boundary-positioned} tool that \emph{captures retrieval signal complementary to (not orthogonal to) trained dense and late-interaction priors}, with the boundary being most pronounced on weak frozen-LLM baselines.

\textbf{Framing of contribution.} This paper is positioned as an \emph{engineering and boundary-characterisation study} rather than a conceptual-breakthrough method paper. No single ingredient (the subspace decomposition, the symmetric averaged-cosine aggregator, graph Laplacian smoothing on a $k$-NN graph, or the supervised fusion-weight regressor) is, taken in isolation, a methodological novelty: product quantization for distance computation has prior literature \citep{jegou2011pq}; symmetric token-level aggregators appear in SBERT-family token similarity, WMD-style optimal transport, and BERTScore; graph Laplacian smoothing on retrieval point-cloud graphs has been used in graph-augmented and graph-of-thought retrieval; supervised per-query fusion weight prediction is a standard learning-to-rank formulation (e.g., LambdaMART). The paper's contribution is the \emph{boundary characterization} (a cosine-strength gradient and a graph-topology dependence that jointly govern when the geometric pipeline helps and when it does not) plus the \emph{systematic falsification taxonomy} (21 falsified approaches---11 with complete raw experimental logs and 10 retrospectively reconstructed with per-entry provenance flagging---organised into three failure-mechanism classes) that surface design constraints not articulated in prior work for the frozen-LLM-token-cloud retrieval regime. Our contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Niche characterization: geometric post-processing for weak-baseline LLM embeddings.} We characterise a specific application niche: when the first-stage cosine baseline is weak (NDCG@10 $<0.3$, typical of frozen general-purpose LLM token-mean centroids), a geometric pipeline whose distance and smoothing operators are training-free and whose per-query fusion weight is supervised by a gradient-boosted (GBM-11) regressor (Section~\ref{subsec:adaptive-lambda}) lifts retrieval quality by $+20.9\%$ (SciFact, against sentence-level cosine $0.4483$; the SciFact gain against the token-level cosine reference $0.4701$ used internally in Table~\ref{tab:ablation-components} is $+15.3\%$) to $+157.4\%$ (FiQA) over the same-encoder cosine baseline. Compared on identical splits to retrieval-trained baselines (BGE-M3, BGE-large-en-v1.5, BGE-reranker-v2-m3, ColBERTv2; audited rebuild after a vector-provenance fix), under the strictly protocol-matched single-split lens (both Ours and baselines on identical single-split single-seed full evaluation; see Section~\ref{subsec:same-infra-ranking}) the pipeline ranks \emph{first} on SCIDOCS ($0.2147$ vs.\ next-best BGE-reranker-v2-m3 $0.1823$, $+17.8\%$), \emph{second} on NFCorpus ($0.3270$ vs.\ $0.3275$, $-0.15\%$), ArguAna ($0.4418$ vs.\ $0.4993$, $-11.5\%$), and FiQA ($0.3977$ vs.\ $0.4227$, $-5.9\%$), and \emph{fourth} on SciFact ($0.4946$, behind ColBERTv2 / BGE-M3 / BGE-reranker, where retrieval-trained cross-encoders dominate on short scientific abstracts). As a robustness cross-check, under our deployment-time 5-seed 50/50 protocol (a less-noisy 5-seed-mean point estimate, but \emph{not} strictly protocol-matched against the single-split single-seed audited baselines) the pipeline ranks first on three of five non-Quora corpora---NFCorpus ($0.3297$ vs.\ $0.3275$, $+0.7\%$), SCIDOCS ($0.2451$ vs.\ $0.1823$, $+34.4\%$), and FiQA ($0.4331$ vs.\ $0.4227$, $+2.5\%$)---second on ArguAna ($0.4862$ vs.\ $0.4993$, $-2.6\%$), and third on SciFact ($0.5418$ vs.\ $0.7228$, $-25.0\%$). Both lenses are reported transparently to forestall a protocol-mismatch concern, with Lens~2 (protocol-matched) as the primary headline and Lens~1 (5-seed deployment) as the robustness cross-check. The contribution is the systematic mapping of \emph{when geometric post-processing helps and when it does not}, with SciFact's residual gap and token-level PQ-Chamfer's $-3.1\%$ on the strong-cosine SciFact baseline (Section~\ref{subsec:boundaries}) serving as direct evidence for the weak-baseline boundary. \textbf{Scope of the ``training-free'' label.} The geometric operators (PQ-Chamfer distance and graph Laplacian smoothing) are training-free; the per-query fusion weight $\lambda$ is learned by a supervised gradient-boosted regressor (GBM-11) on per-query oracle $\lambda^\star$ targets computed from \texttt{qrels} under a 50/50 train/test split (Section~\ref{subsec:adaptive-lambda}). Deployment-time alternatives that do not require oracle $\lambda^\star$ are explored as a LinUCB online policy on the single NFCorpus corpus as a proof-of-concept; full deployment-time online learning is identified as future work.

\item \textbf{Geometric post-processing pipeline (two-stage core on frozen-LLM token point clouds, plus an optional centroid coarse filter).} Two core geometric operations are composed on per-token hidden states from a frozen Qwen3-8B (\texttt{--pooling none}; document point clouds with median ${\sim}356$ tokens, no retrieval-specific training) and characterize their behaviour as a niche post-processing layer rather than as a method applicable to all encoder regimes: (a) \emph{PQ-Chamfer distance}---we use product quantization \citep{jegou2011pq} from a compression primitive to a distance primitive\footnote{Chamfer distance is a semimetric (Lemma~\ref{lem:pq-chamfer-semimetric}); we use ``distance'' following point cloud convention.}, decomposing $\R^{4096} = \bigoplus_{s=1}^{64} \R^{64}$, computing exact subspace cosine distances and averaging across $M=64$ subspaces; this breaks concentration of measure with no quantization error (measured $\sigma_{\rm sub}/\sigma_{\rm full}$ ratios $2.0\times$/$1.61\times$/$1.54\times$ for Qwen3-8B/BGE-M3/BGE-large, audited rebuild; Section~\ref{subsec:concentration-measurement}); geometrically, MaxSim ``votes for the best per-token match'' (asymmetric, full-dimensional) while PQ-Chamfer ``recognizes the overall geometric shape'' via subspace-decomposed averaged distance (symmetric, subspace-projected); (b) \emph{token-level point cloud retrieval on frozen LLM hidden states}---PQ-Chamfer is applied directly to per-token hidden states without retrieval-specific fine-tuning, distinguishing the operator from ColBERT/ColBERTv2 \citep{khattab2020colbert,santhanam2022colbertv2} which require a trained token-aware encoder; (c) \emph{graph Laplacian smoothing} on a $k$-NN document graph built from PQ-Chamfer distances (not centroid cosine), iterated as $C^{(t+1)} = (I - \alpha L) C^{(t)}$. On the same-encoder Qwen3-8B configuration, graph Laplacian smoothing yields $+5.8\%$ to $+44.2\%$ NDCG@10 on all six BEIR datasets evaluated (NFCorpus, ArguAna, SciFact, SCIDOCS, FiQA, and Quora) relative to the same-encoder cosine baseline; the five non-Quora corpora carry the GBM-11 deployment-rule headline because token-level Qwen3-8B point cloud data is fully available on those corpora. Cross-encoder applicability is more nuanced: under cross-model per-(corpus, model) tuning (Section~\ref{subsec:cross-model}), smoothing helps on $1$ of $5$ corpus--model pairs in our heterogeneous-encoder grid and degrades on the others; the smoother is therefore characterised as same-encoder consistent and cross-encoder tuning-required---a regime-specific tool requiring per-(model, corpus) tuning.

\item \textbf{Supervised per-query fusion weight on top of the training-free geometric operators.} The manually-set fusion weight is replaced $\lambda$ with a gradient-boosted regressor (GBM, $11$ features) that predicts $\lambda$ from cheap per-query proxies. Macro NDCG@10 across the five BEIR corpora rises from $0.3653$ (fixed $\lambda=0.3$) to $0.4072$ ($+11.5\%$ relative-to-fixed; Section~\ref{subsec:adaptive-lambda}), closing $69\%$ of the oracle--fixed gap. The training data are per-query oracle $\lambda^\star$ targets computed from \texttt{qrels} under a 50/50 train/test split. The remaining oracle gap motivates an online incremental-SGD extension that is sketched in Section~\ref{subsec:adaptive-lambda} and report a proof-of-concept LinUCB replay run for on NFCorpus (single-corpus PoC); full deployment-time online learning is identified as future work. We also explore a forward, training-free Hartree-style self-consistent variant (\textbf{2 free parameters} versus \textbf{11} for GBM-11; Section~\ref{subsec:adaptive-lambda}, Eqn.~\eqref{eq:hartree-self-consistent}) that determines per-query $\lambda^{\rm eff}(q)$ as a fixed point of the retrieval-norm closure without requiring oracle $\lambda^\star$ supervision; this variant matches fixed $\lambda=0.3$ on NFCorpus ($0.3106$ vs $0.3116$) but underperforms GBM-11 ($0.3297$) by $5.8\%$ relative, reported transparently as a negative result that establishes the $11\to 2$ free-parameter reduction is achievable in form but not at parity retrieval quality. The supervised fusion weight is the \emph{only} learned component of the pipeline; the distance and smoothing operators remain training-free.

\item \textbf{Systematic falsification with a three-class failure-mechanism taxonomy.} We document 21 falsified approaches---\textbf{11 with complete raw experimental logs preserved from the development of this research program (per-query NDCG@10, configuration, notes); 10 retrospectively reconstructed from contemporaneous notes with provenance flagged per-entry} (Supplementary Material~A.3). The 21 entries are organized into three failure-mechanism classes---curse-of-orthogonality / random-projection failures; saturation-on-strong-baseline failures; signal-cancellation / information-redundancy failures---with mathematical mechanism analysis per class. Together with the boundary characterization (item~1) this falsification taxonomy is the paper's main substantive contribution: it surfaces design constraints (the cosine-strength gradient, the graph-topology dependence, the saturation-on-strong-baseline barrier) that any geometric post-processor on frozen-LLM token clouds must respect. Audit-grade reproducibility---including the independent vector-provenance audit that revealed $19.93\%$/$18.62\%$ of NFCorpus/SciFact corpus documents in earlier BGE-M3 vector exports were silently zero-encoded (root cause and Pearson $r\approx0.998$ propagation analysis in Section~\ref{subsec:cross-model} \emph{Vector-provenance note}; all baseline numbers in Table~\ref{tab:agent-b-baselines} are the audited rebuild)---is reported as a methodological commitment in Section~\ref{subsec:cross-model} rather than as a stand-alone contribution slot. Code, indices, and the full falsification log are released for anonymous review at \url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/SHAPE-CFD-IS2026/}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Research Objectives}
\label{subsec:research-objectives}

This study addresses the following research questions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{RQ1:} Can training-free geometric operations (PQ-Chamfer distance and graph Laplacian smoothing), composed with a supervised per-query fusion-weight predictor, on general-purpose LLM hidden states improve dense retrieval quality?
\item \textbf{RQ2:} How does the improvement transfer across different frozen embedding architectures (Qwen3-8B-Q4, BGE-M3, BGE-large-en-v1.5), and what are the boundaries of cross-encoder applicability under per-(corpus, model) tuning?
\item \textbf{RQ3:} At what computational cost can these improvements be achieved compared to training specialized retrieval models?
\end{enumerate}

\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================

The work is positioned within five intersecting strands of prior research, then explicitly delineate the regime in which our specific design choices are, to our knowledge, new.

\subsection{Multi-Vector and Point-Cloud Document Representations}
\label{subsec:related-multivec}

The idea of representing documents as point clouds---collections of token-level or phrase-level vectors rather than a single centroid---is not new in information retrieval. Prior work is organised along the dimension that most distinguishes it from ours: whether the representation is \emph{trained} or \emph{frozen}.

\textbf{Trained multi-vector retrieval.} The dominant family is built around contrastive fine-tuning of token-aware encoders. ColBERT~\citep{khattab2020colbert} introduced late interaction with the asymmetric MaxSim operator $\mathrm{MaxSim}(q, d) = \sum_{i \in q} \max_{j \in d} \langle q_i, d_j\rangle$ on top of a BERT encoder fine-tuned on MSMARCO query--document pairs. ColBERTv2~\citep{santhanam2022colbertv2} compressed token embeddings via residual quantization with learned codebooks, and PLAID~\citep{santhanam2022plaid} accelerated search through centroid-based candidate pruning. COIL~\citep{gao2021coil} restricted late interaction to lexically-overlapping tokens, reducing token-token comparisons. ME-BERT~\citep{luan2021mebert} pooled multiple cluster centroids per document instead of a single CLS vector. DESSERT~\citep{engels2024dessert} accelerated multi-vector retrieval via locality-sensitive hashing on token vectors. All of these methods share two properties that distinguish them from our work: (i)~the token-level encoder is contrastively fine-tuned on retrieval supervision, and (ii)~the retrieval-time scoring uses an asymmetric or dot-product formulation rather than a symmetric subspace-averaged cosine.

\textbf{Frozen multi-vector representations.} A smaller line of work explores point-cloud retrieval on frozen encoders. Sentence-BERT~\citep{reimers2019sbert} demonstrated that frozen pretrained encoders, when pooled appropriately, achieve competitive retrieval; however, the pooling collapses the document to a single vector and is not a point cloud. WMD~\citep{kusner2015wmd} computed earth mover's distance between word-embedding clouds of two documents, but on word2vec rather than transformer hidden states and without graph regularization. BERTScore~\citep{zhang2020bertscore} applied one-directional max-pooling between two BERT-encoded sentences for evaluation rather than retrieval ranking. To our knowledge, no prior work computes (a)~symmetric subspace-decomposed Chamfer distance on (b)~frozen general-purpose LLM hidden states for (c)~document retrieval ranking with (d)~an additional inter-document graph regularization step. Our contribution is the specific combination, not any single ingredient.

\textbf{Recent LLM-based embeddings.} E5-Mistral~\citep{wang2024e5mistral} and GTE~\citep{li2024gte} fine-tune large LLMs on retrieval objectives to produce strong single-vector representations, but they collapse the per-token cloud into one centroid via mean pooling and still require contrastive training. Any such encoder is treated as a frozen feature extractor and apply geometric reranking on top of it; results across Qwen3-8B-Q4 (4096-d, frozen), BGE-large-en-v1.5 (1024-d, contrastive-trained, frozen at our use point), and BGE-M3 (1024-d, contrastive-trained, frozen) are reported in Section~\ref{subsec:cross-model}.

\subsection{Late-Interaction Comparison Axes}
\label{subsec:related-late}

Three structural axes pin down on which our pipeline differs from the ColBERT family:

\textbf{(i) Representation source.} Frozen general-purpose LLM hidden states are used (Qwen3, BGE, GTE) with no retrieval-specific contrastive training; ColBERT-family encoders are explicitly trained on query--document supervision.

\textbf{(ii) Aggregation symmetry and metric structure.} PQ-Chamfer is symmetric and operates on \emph{subspace cosine distances} averaged across $M=64$ subspaces:
\[
d_{\mathrm{PQ}}(q, d) = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{s=1}^{M} \delta_s\bigl(q^{(s)}, d^{(s)}\bigr),
\]
with $\delta_s$ the cosine distance in subspace $s$ (Section~\ref{sec:method}). MaxSim is asymmetric, dot-product based, and operates on full-dimensional vectors. The subspace average breaks concentration of measure: the isotropic-Gaussian textbook prediction is a $7.8\times$ discrimination gain (from ${\sim}0.016$ in $\R^{4096}$ to ${\sim}0.125$ per subspace), but the empirically measured $\sigma_{\rm sub}/\sigma_{\rm full}$ ratio on contextual LLM embeddings is $2.0\times$ on Qwen3-8B / $1.61\times$ on BGE-M3 / $1.54\times$ on BGE-large (anisotropy depresses the gain; see Section~\ref{subsec:concentration-measurement}). MaxSim does not exploit either subspace structure.

\textbf{(iii) Inter-document signal.} A graph Laplacian smoothing step is introduced that no late-interaction method performs: ColBERT-family scoring is per-pair (independent of other documents), whereas our pipeline additionally propagates relevance across a document KNN graph built from PQ-Chamfer distances.

\subsection{Two Structural Novelties}
\label{subsec:related-novelties}

Two design choices are, to our knowledge, novel in this combination, and they jointly anchor our positioning:

\textbf{(N1) PQ \emph{without} quantization, as an exact distance.} Product quantization \citep{jegou2011pq} is canonically a \emph{compression} primitive: subspace decomposition $\R^{D} = \bigoplus_s \R^{D/M}$ is paired with per-subspace $K$-means codebooks to store quantized codes for approximate-nearest-neighbor search. ColBERTv2/PLAID inherit this trade-off (smaller storage, quantization-error penalty). The trade-off is inverted: the subspace decomposition is kept and \emph{drop} the codebook, computing exact cosine distance within each subspace and averaging across subspaces. The benefit is variance inflation without quantization error; the cost is full-precision token storage. Quantization-as-storage and our distance-as-decomposition are complementary rather than competing; an integrated PQ-codebook variant of our pipeline is identified as a future-work direction (Section~\ref{subsec:future-work}).

\textbf{(N2) Graph Laplacian on a point-cloud-distance KNN graph.} Standard graph reranking methods such as G-RAG~\citep{glass2024grag} or GAR~\citep{macavaney2022gar} build their KNN graphs from \emph{single-vector similarities} (centroid cosine or BM25). The graph is built from \emph{point-cloud distances} (PQ-Chamfer between document token clouds), giving the smoothing operator a graph topology that respects sub-document structure. To our knowledge, no prior graph-reranking method uses a token-level point-cloud distance to define graph edges.

\textbf{LLM-based reranking.} RankGPT \citep{sun2024rankgpt} demonstrates that LLMs can serve as effective listwise rerankers via prompt engineering. RankLLM \citep{pradeep2025rankllm} provides a scalable framework for LLM-based reranking. AFR-Rank \citep{afrrank2025} improves efficiency by filtering noise documents before LLM reranking. A recent survey \citep{li2025reranking_survey} traces the evolution from heuristic reranking to LLM-based approaches. These methods achieve strong results but require expensive API calls (\$0.01--0.10 per query); our pipeline---training-free geometric operators with a supervised per-query fusion-weight predictor (Section~\ref{subsec:adaptive-lambda})---achieves competitive results at orders of magnitude lower cost in the weak-baseline LLM embedding niche.

\textbf{Graph-based reranking.} Graph methods span from PageRank \citep{page1999pagerank} to diffusion-based RAG approaches \citep{dampanaboina2024diffrag}. G-RAG \citep{glass2024grag} learns graph neural network rerankers, and GAR \citep{macavaney2022gar} uses pseudo-relevance feedback on document graphs. Our Laplacian smoothing operates on point-cloud-derived distances and requires no training.

\textbf{Product quantization.} PQ \citep{jegou2011pq} and systems built on it (FAISS \citep{johnson2021faiss}, ScaNN \citep{guo2020scann}, OPQ \citep{ge2014opq}) use subspace decomposition for compression and approximate search. The decomposition is repurposed for exact distance computation, using subspace structure to break concentration of measure.

\textbf{Point cloud distances.} Chamfer distance \citep{fan2017chamfer} and EMD \citep{rubner2000emd} are the dominant 3D point cloud metrics. Chamfer corresponds to unbalanced optimal transport with fully relaxed marginals \citep{sejourne2023uot}, making it effective for documents of different lengths. BERTScore \citep{zhang2020bertscore} applies a similar one-directional matching; WMD \citep{kusner2015wmd} uses EMD over word embeddings.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Document Point Cloud Representation}
\label{subsec:point-cloud}

Let $\mathcal{M}$ denote a pretrained language model (Qwen3-8B, $d = 4096$). For a document $d_i$ consisting of tokens $\{t_{i,1}, t_{i,2}, \ldots, t_{i,n_i}\}$, the last hidden states are extracted with pooling disabled (\texttt{--pooling none}):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:point-cloud}
\mathcal{P}_i = \{h_{i,1}, h_{i,2}, \ldots, h_{i,n_i}\} \subset \R^d
\end{equation}
where $h_{i,j} \in \R^{4096}$ is the last-layer hidden state for token $t_{i,j}$. Similarly, a query $q$ with tokens $\{t_{q,1}, \ldots, t_{q,m}\}$ yields a query point cloud $\mathcal{P}_q = \{h_{q,1}, \ldots, h_{q,m}\}$.

In our NFCorpus evaluation, the average document contains ${\sim}356$ tokens and the average query contains ${\sim}6$ tokens. The entire NFCorpus token cloud database (2{,}473 documents, 880{,}791 tokens) occupies 14\,GB in SQLite storage.

\textbf{Centroid representation.} For coarse-grained operations, the centroid is also computed of each point cloud:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:centroid}
\bv_i = \frac{1}{n_i} \sum_{j=1}^{n_i} h_{i,j}
\end{equation}

This centroid serves as the single-vector representation for initial retrieval and coarse filtering.

\subsection{PQ-Chamfer Distance}
\label{subsec:pq-chamfer}

PQ-Chamfer distance is defined in four stages, presented incrementally below: (i) subspace decomposition of the ambient embedding space; (ii) Chamfer aggregation across two point clouds; (iii) a boundedness lemma establishing the operator's metric structure; and (iv) a VT-Aligned variant that reverses the aggregation order. Each stage introduces one operator with two-to-three sentences of intuition before its formal definition.

\textbf{Stage~I. Subspace decomposition of the ambient embedding space.} The geometric reason for splitting the $4096$-d space is concentration-of-measure: in high ambient dimension cosines collapse toward a narrow band and lose discriminative resolution, whereas $64$-d sub-cosines retain spread (measured in Section~\ref{subsec:concentration-measurement}). Concretely, the $4096$-dimensional embedding space is partitioned into $M = 64$ contiguous subspaces of dimension $d_s = 64$ each. For a vector $h \in \R^{4096}$, we write $h^{(s)}$ for the projection onto the $s$-th subspace:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:subspace}
h^{(s)} = (h_{(s-1)d_s+1}, \ldots, h_{s \cdot d_s}) \in \mathbb{R}^{d_s}, \quad s = 1, \ldots, M
\end{equation}

The cosine distance within the $s$-th subspace is denoted $\delta_s$ to distinguish it from the subspace dimension $d_s$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:subspace-dist}
\delta_s(a, b) = 1 - \frac{a^{(s)} \cdot b^{(s)}}{\|a^{(s)}\| \, \|b^{(s)}\|}
\end{equation}

The PQ distance between two tokens is the average across subspaces:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pq-dist}
d_{\mathrm{PQ}}(a, b) = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{s=1}^{M} \delta_s(a, b)
\end{equation}

\textbf{Why subspace decomposition works (measured, not assumed).} The isotropic-Gaussian textbook prediction is $\sigma \approx 1/\sqrt{d} = 0.016$ for $d=4096$ and $\sigma_s \approx 1/\sqrt{64} = 0.125$ for $d_s=64$, giving a discrimination ratio of $\sqrt{M} = 8.0\times$. However, contextual LLM embeddings are well-documented to be \emph{anisotropic} \citep{ethayarajh2019contextual,cai2021isotropy}, so the isotropic prediction does not directly apply.

\paragraph{Concentration-of-measure: empirical anisotropy measurement.}\label{subsec:concentration-measurement} We characterise pairwise-cosine standard deviation on Qwen3-8B sentence-level centroid embeddings from NFCorpus, using 2{,}000 documents and 10{,}000 random pairs per measurement (\texttt{benchmark/measure\_sigma\_subspace.py}). The full 4096-d cosine has mean $= 0.76$, indicating substantial anisotropy (an isotropic Gaussian gives mean near zero), and $\sigma_{\rm full} = 0.058$. The per-subspace 64-d cosine, averaged across all 64 contiguous subspaces, gives $\sigma_{\rm sub} = 0.118$. This matches the isotropic-subspace prediction of $0.125$ within $5\%$, with per-subspace $\sigma$ ranging $[0.027, 0.195]$. The measured discrimination ratio is $\sigma_{\rm sub}/\sigma_{\rm full} = 2.0\times$, lower than the isotropic prediction of $8.0\times$ but strictly positive. Subspace decomposition does break the global anisotropy in practice, just less dramatically than isotropic Gaussian theory suggests. Note that this is not a random projection (which proved harmful in our tests; see Section~\ref{subsec:falsification}): the subspace boundaries respect the model's dimensional structure. Ablation confirms that contiguous $64 \times 64$ outperforms $32 \times 128$ and $16 \times 256$. Ordered subspaces perform comparably to randomly permuted ones. This indicates that Qwen3's embedding dimensions are $\approx$ uniform in \emph{within-subspace} information content, even though the global geometry is anisotropic. Full output: \texttt{benchmark/data/results/sigma\_subspace\_measurement.json}.

\textbf{Cross-model anisotropy verification (audited rebuild).} The same measurement was repeated on BGE-M3 (audited-rebuild vectors, see Section~\ref{subsec:cross-model} \textit{Vector-provenance note}) and BGE-large-en-v1.5 NFCorpus corpus embeddings (each $1024$-d, partitioned into $M=16$ contiguous $64$-d subspaces). Results: BGE-M3 $\sigma_{\rm sub}/\sigma_{\rm full} = \mathbf{1.61\times}$ (full $\sigma = 0.068$, subspace mean $\sigma = 0.110$, audited rebuild post zero-vector fix); BGE-large $\sigma_{\rm sub}/\sigma_{\rm full} = 1.54\times$ (full $\sigma = 0.063$, subspace mean $\sigma = 0.097$). Both are below the corresponding isotropic prediction $\sqrt{16} = 4.0\times$ but strictly positive --- confirming that the anisotropy plus subspace-discrimination pattern is \emph{consistent across LLM-derived dense encoders} (Qwen3-8B 4096-d $\sigma_{\rm sub}/\sigma_{\rm full} = 2.0\times$, BGE-M3 NFCorpus $1.61\times$ [5-corpus mean $1.66\times$, range $1.57$--$1.80\times$; see Table~\ref{tab:sigma-cross-corpus-model}], BGE-large NFCorpus $1.54\times$), not a Qwen3-specific phenomenon. The pre-audit BGE-M3 ratio reported in earlier drafts ($2.70\times$) was inflated by 19.93\% silently zero-encoded NFCorpus corpus documents in the prior BGE-M3 vectors, which depressed full-cosine $\sigma$ relative to subspace $\sigma$; the audited rebuild gives the correct, more conservative value. Per-model output: \texttt{benchmark/data/results/sigma\_bge\_m3\_v2\_nfcorpus.json} (audited), \texttt{sigma\_bge\_large\_nfcorpus.json}.

With the per-token PQ distance \eqref{eq:pq-dist} in place, we now lift it to a distance between point clouds via Chamfer aggregation.

\textbf{Stage~II. Chamfer aggregation across point clouds.} Given two point clouds $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{B}$, the PQ-Chamfer distance is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:chamfer}
d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B}) = \frac{1}{2} \left[ \frac{1}{|\mathcal{A}|} \sum_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \min_{b \in \mathcal{B}} d_{\mathrm{PQ}}(a, b) + \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|} \sum_{b \in \mathcal{B}} \min_{a \in \mathcal{A}} d_{\mathrm{PQ}}(a, b) \right]
\end{equation}

The forward term $\frac{1}{|\mathcal{A}|}\sum_a \min_b$ measures how well $\mathcal{B}$ \textit{covers} $\mathcal{A}$ (every point in $\mathcal{A}$ finds a nearby match in $\mathcal{B}$), and the backward term ensures symmetry.

\textbf{Stage~III. Boundedness and metric structure.} Before Chamfer aggregation can be used inside a graph Laplacian smoothing operator (Section~\ref{subsec:graph-smoothing}), we record the four metric properties that the smoothing argument actually requires.

\begin{lemma}[PQ-Chamfer is a bounded symmetric semimetric, not a metric]
\label{lem:pq-chamfer-semimetric}
For any non-empty point clouds $\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^{d}$ with $d = M \cdot d_s$, the PQ-Chamfer distance defined in Eq.~\eqref{eq:chamfer} satisfies:
\textup{(i) Boundedness:} $d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B}) \in [0, 2]$;
\textup{(ii) Symmetry:} $d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B}) = d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{B}, \mathcal{A})$;
\textup{(iii) Reflexivity:} $d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{A}) = 0$;
\textup{(iv) Triangle inequality does \emph{not} hold in general.}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
(i) Each $\delta_s(a,b) = 1 - \cos\angle(a^{(s)}, b^{(s)}) \in [0, 2]$, so $d_{\mathrm{PQ}}(a,b) = \tfrac{1}{M}\sum_s \delta_s \in [0, 2]$. The Chamfer aggregation in Eq.~\eqref{eq:chamfer} averages forward and backward $\min$ terms, each itself in $[0, 2]$, giving $d_{\mathrm{PQC}} \in [0, 2]$.
(ii) The half-sum in Eq.~\eqref{eq:chamfer} is symmetric by construction: swapping $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{B}$ swaps the two halves.
(iii) For $a \in \mathcal{A}$, $\min_{b \in \mathcal{A}} d_{\mathrm{PQ}}(a, b) = d_{\mathrm{PQ}}(a, a) = 0$ (taking $b = a$); both halves of Eq.~\eqref{eq:chamfer} reduce to zero.
(iv) Counter-example: take $\mathcal{A} = \{e_1\}$, $\mathcal{B} = \{(e_1 + e_2)/\sqrt{2}\}$, $\mathcal{C} = \{e_2\}$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$ (extended trivially to $\mathbb{R}^{d}$). Then $d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{A},\mathcal{B}) = d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{B},\mathcal{C}) = 1 - 1/\sqrt{2} \approx 0.293$, while $d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{A},\mathcal{C}) = 1 > 2 \cdot 0.293 = 0.586$, violating $d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{A},\mathcal{C}) \le d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{A},\mathcal{B}) + d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{B},\mathcal{C})$.
\end{proof}

\noindent The KNN graph and Laplacian smoothing (Section~\ref{subsec:graph-smoothing}) treat $d_{\mathrm{PQC}}$ as a \emph{similarity proxy} rather than a true metric: the spectral argument used in Section~\ref{subsec:graph-smoothing} requires only that the symmetric edge weight $W_{ij} = \exp(-\beta d_{ij})$ be non-negative and bounded, both of which follow from properties (i)--(ii) of Lemma~\ref{lem:pq-chamfer-semimetric}. Retrieval ranking requires only isotonicity of $d_{\mathrm{PQC}}$ with respect to relevance, not full metric structure (e.g., dot-product similarity, the de facto IR ranking signal, is also not a metric).

\textbf{Stage~IV. VT-Aligned variant (aggregation-order swap).} Stages~I--III define the operator used in our pipeline. We complete the four-stage map by recording one variant we ablated and dropped, so the design space surrounding PQ-Chamfer is explicit. The standard PQ-Chamfer computes subspace distances first and then applies the Chamfer $\min$ operator (``average-then-min''):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:avg-then-min}
d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B}) = \text{Chamfer}\!\left(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B};\; d_{\mathrm{PQ}}(a,b) = \tfrac{1}{M}\textstyle\sum_s \delta_s(a,b)\right)
\end{equation}

VT-Aligned reverses the aggregation order (``min-then-average''): the minimization over the target set is performed \emph{independently within each subspace}, allowing different subspaces to select different nearest neighbors:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:vt-aligned}
d_{\mathrm{VT}}(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B}) = \frac{1}{2}\left[\frac{1}{|\mathcal{A}|}\sum_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \frac{1}{M}\sum_{s=1}^{M} \min_{b \in \mathcal{B}} \delta_s(a, b) + \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}\sum_{b \in \mathcal{B}} \frac{1}{M}\sum_{s=1}^{M} \min_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \delta_s(b, a)\right]
\end{equation}

This captures finer-grained matching patterns than standard PQ-Chamfer, where a single globally nearest neighbor is used across all subspaces. Early ablation observed comparable performance for the VT-Aligned variant; the variant is omitted from the present pipeline due to lack of hypothesis-tested significance on the VT-Aligned vs.\ standard PQ-Chamfer contrast.

\textbf{Connection to unbalanced optimal transport.} Chamfer distance corresponds to UOT with fully relaxed marginals ($\tau \to 0$) \citep{sejourne2023uot}. This relaxation is essential for documents of different lengths: a 6-token query matched against a 356-token document need not force all document tokens to match, functioning as \textit{hard semantic attention}.

\subsection{Graph Laplacian Smoothing}
\label{subsec:graph-smoothing}

Given a set of $N$ candidate documents with initial relevance scores, a weighted KNN graph is constructed and iteratively smoothed.

\textbf{Graph construction.} A sparse undirected graph is built $\mathcal{G} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E}, W)$ over the $N$ candidates:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textit{Pairwise distance:} Compute $d_{ij} = d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{P}_i, \mathcal{P}_j)$ for all pairs, or use VT-Aligned distance.
\item \textit{KNN sparsification:} For each node $i$, retain edges to its $K = 3$ nearest neighbors.
\item \textit{Edge weights:} $W_{ij} = \exp(-\beta \cdot d_{ij})$, with $\beta = 2.0$.
\item \textit{Symmetrization:} If $j \in \mathcal{N}(i)$ but $i \notin \mathcal{N}(j)$, add the reverse edge with $W_{ji} = W_{ij}$.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Initial scores.} Each document receives an initial ``concentration'':
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:init-score}
C_0(i) = \exp(-2 \cdot d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{P}_q, \mathcal{P}_i))
\end{equation}

\textbf{Laplacian smoothing iteration.} The score update follows the discrete heat equation on the graph:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:laplacian-update}
C_{t+1}(i) = C_t(i) + \alpha \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)} W_{ij} \left( C_t(j) - C_t(i) \right)
\end{equation}
where $\alpha = 0.15$ is the diffusion coefficient. $T = 5$ iterations are used. This can be written in matrix form as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:matrix-form}
C_{t+1} = (I - \alpha L)\, C_t
\end{equation}
where $L = D_{\mathrm{deg}} - W$ is the graph Laplacian and $D_{\mathrm{deg}}$ is the degree matrix.

\textbf{Stability condition.} The eigenvalues of the update matrix $(I - \alpha L)$ lie in $[0,1]$ iff $\alpha \le 2/\lambda_{\max}(L)$, where $L = D - W$ is the graph Laplacian. For weighted graphs, $\lambda_{\max}(L) \le 2 d_{\max}^{(W)}$ where $d_{\max}^{(W)} = \max_i \sum_j W_{ij}$ is the maximum weighted degree (this is tight for bipartite graphs; otherwise loose). The sufficient condition $\alpha \le 1/d_{\max}^{(W)}$ follows from $\lambda_{\max}(L) \le 2 d_{\max}^{(W)}$. Since edge weights $W_{ij} = \exp(-\beta d_{ij}) \in (0, 1]$, we have $d_{\max}^{(W)} \le d_{\max}^{(\text{unweighted})}$, the unweighted degree. For $K = 3$ with bidirectional symmetrization, the unweighted degree is at most $2K = 6$, so $\alpha \le 1/6 \approx 0.167$ suffices. Our choice $\alpha = 0.15$ satisfies this sufficient condition. Empirically the tighter bound $\alpha \le 2/\lambda_{\max}(L)$ is rarely binding because $\lambda_{\max}(L) < 2 d_{\max}^{(W)}$ on most KNN graphs we observed.

\textbf{Effect.} Laplacian smoothing propagates high relevance scores to semantically similar neighbors, effectively discovering relevant documents that may have received low initial scores due to vocabulary mismatch or embedding noise. Unlike cross-encoder reranking, this propagation is training-free and operates in milliseconds.

\subsection{Multi-Granularity Pipeline}
\label{subsec:pipeline}

Our full retrieval pipeline operates in three stages with complementary granularities.

\textbf{Stage~0: Centroid coarse filtering.} Compute cosine similarity between the query centroid $\bv_q$ and all document centroids $\{\bv_i\}$ in the corpus. Retain the top-$K_1 = 100$ candidates. This stage runs in 23\,ms on commodity CPU hardware.

\textbf{Stage~1: Token PQ-Chamfer reranking.} For the top-100 candidates, compute the full token-level PQ-Chamfer distance $d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{P}_q, \mathcal{P}_i)$ using all token hidden states. Re-rank and retain the top-$K_2 = 55$ candidates. This stage runs in 23\,ms.

\textbf{Stage~2: Graph Laplacian smoothing.} Construct a KNN graph over the top-55 candidates and apply 5 iterations of Laplacian smoothing as described in Section~\ref{subsec:graph-smoothing}. This stage runs in 26\,ms.

\textbf{Score fusion.} For datasets where token-level data is available (NFCorpus, SciFact, ArguAna, SCIDOCS, FiQA), the two signal sources are combined:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fusion}
\text{score}_{\text{final}}(i) = (1 - \lambda) \cdot \text{score}_{\text{token}}(i) + \lambda \cdot \text{score}_{\text{graph}}(i)
\end{equation}
where $\lambda \in [0,1]$ trades off pure token matching ($\lambda=0$) against pure graph propagation ($\lambda=1$). For Table~\ref{tab:main} we report the per-dataset optimum $\lambda^\star$ on all five datasets with token-level data (NFCorpus $\lambda^\star=0.4$, ArguAna $\lambda^\star=0.0$, SciFact $\lambda^\star=0.7$, SCIDOCS $\lambda^\star=0.2$, FiQA $\lambda^\star=0.2$). Both score vectors are min-max normalized before fusion. The dataset-dependence of $\lambda^\star$ and complete $\lambda$ sweep are analyzed in detail in Section~\ref{subsec:fusion-mechanism}.

\textbf{Two-stage acceleration is lossless.} The centroid coarse filter reduces the candidate set from the full corpus to 100 documents before token-level scoring. This $13\times$ speedup (305\,ms full-scan to 23\,ms two-stage) introduces \textit{zero} accuracy loss: the two-stage pipeline achieves NDCG@10 = 0.3220 versus 0.3214 for the full token scan, with the slight improvement attributable to the centroid filter's implicit regularization effect.

\textbf{Complexity analysis.} Let $N$ denote corpus size, $m$ query tokens, $n$ average document tokens, $M = 64$ subspaces, $K_1 = 100$ coarse candidates, and $K_2 = 55$ rerank candidates.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textit{Stage~0 (centroid coarse filtering):} $O(N \cdot d)$ cosine comparisons, or $O(N \cdot m \cdot M)$ if using PQ-Chamfer at centroid level.
\item \textit{Stage~1 (token PQ-Chamfer reranking):} $O(K_1 \cdot m \cdot n \cdot M)$ per query---for each of $K_1$ candidates, compute $m \times n$ token-pair distances across $M$ subspaces.
\item \textit{Stage~2 (graph Laplacian smoothing):} $O(K_2^2 \cdot n^2 \cdot M)$ for pairwise graph construction $+\; O(T \cdot K_2 \cdot K)$ for $T$ smoothing iterations on a $K$-sparse graph.
\end{itemize}

With typical NFCorpus parameters ($m \approx 6$, $n \approx 356$, $K_1 = 100$, $K_2 = 55$, $M = 64$), the bottleneck is Stage~1 at ${\sim}137$M floating-point operations per query, completed in 23\,ms on commodity CPU.

\textbf{Baselines.} (1)~Cosine similarity (single-vector retrieval), (2)~BM25 (sparse retrieval), (3)~an earlier-iteration convection-diffusion baseline on sentence-level point clouds (denoted in our internal logs as a prior PDE-style approach). We also contextualize our results against published numbers from trained retrieval models (ColBERTv2, BGE-large-en-v1.5) and LLM-based rerankers (RankGPT \citep{sun2024rankgpt}, RankLLM \citep{pradeep2025rankllm}). LLM-based rerankers such as RankGPT achieve state-of-the-art results on BEIR benchmarks but require expensive API calls (\$0.01--0.10 per query); AFR-Rank \citep{afrrank2025} mitigates this cost through noise document filtering. Our pipeline---training-free geometric operators with a supervised per-query fusion-weight predictor---achieves competitive results at orders of magnitude lower cost (\$47 total for all experiments).

\textbf{Metric.} NDCG@10, the standard metric for BEIR evaluation. Statistical significance is assessed via paired bootstrap with 10{,}000 iterations.

Three patterns are observed under the \emph{same-encoder Qwen3-8B-Q4 configuration} (cross-encoder transfer is discussed separately in Section~\ref{subsec:cross-model} and bounded in Section~\ref{subsec:boundaries}). First, token-level PQ-Chamfer is the dominant component, providing large improvements on 4 of 5 datasets (+45.0\% to +131.5\%) with the sole exception of SciFact ($-3.1\%$), where the strong cosine baseline (0.4483) limits available headroom. Second, graph Laplacian smoothing is the most consistent single improvement \emph{within the same-encoder Qwen3 regime}, outperforming cosine on all six BEIR datasets evaluated (NFCorpus, ArguAna, SciFact, SCIDOCS, FiQA, and Quora; +5.8\% to +44.2\%); cross-encoder transfer is more nuanced and requires per-(model, corpus) tuning (Section~\ref{subsec:cross-model}). Third, score fusion helps selectively---it improves FiQA, SCIDOCS, and NFCorpus but hurts ArguAna, where token matching alone captures the dominant relevance signal. Pure Laplacian smoothing also outperforms the full convection-diffusion PDE on 5 of 6 datasets, which is the basis for retiring the advection formulation (Section~\ref{subsec:falsification}).

\subsection{Statistical Significance and Identical-Split Baselines}\label{subsec:significance}
Statistical significance is reported on two reference configurations.

\subparagraph{(a) Per-corpus oracle $\lambda^\star$ reference.} Paired bootstrap (10{,}000 iterations, seed 42) over per-query NDCG@10 compares \emph{Oracle Best} (per-corpus $\lambda^\star$, Table~\ref{tab:ablation-components}) against \emph{Cosine} on all five BEIR datasets with token-level data. The resulting two-sided $p$-values are robust under 51-seed replication. \textbf{Four of five corpora} (NFCorpus, ArguAna, SCIDOCS, FiQA) report $p < 0.001$ with $p_{\max}$ across 51 seeds $= 0.0001$. \textbf{SciFact reports $p < 0.005$} with $p_{\max} = 0.0014$, the only corpus where the single-seed $p < 0.001$ claim is borderline. Cohen's $d$ ranges from $0.197$ (SciFact) to $0.717$ (FiQA) and is stable across seeds (per-corpus $d$ standard deviation $< 0.001$). Per-corpus $\Delta$NDCG@10 with 95\% CI and $d$: NFCorpus $+0.0970$ ($[+0.078, +0.117]$, $d=0.552$); ArguAna $+0.1370$ ($[+0.121, +0.153]$, $d=0.443$); SciFact $+0.0462$ ($[+0.020, +0.073]$, $d=0.197$); SCIDOCS $+0.1072$ ($[+0.095, +0.119]$, $d=0.562$); FiQA $+0.2293$ ($[+0.205, +0.254]$, $d=0.717$). The 51-seed robustness check (50 seeds 0--49 plus seed 42) rules out single-seed bootstrap volatility as a confounder. \subparagraph{(b) GBM-11 deployment-time reference.} Under the supervised GBM-11 fusion-weight predictor (5-seed train/test 50/50 split mean), per-corpus NDCG@10 lies within $4.0$--$6.1\%$ of the per-corpus 5-seed-mean oracle upper bound on each of the 5 corpora (Table~\ref{tab:ablation-components}). Macro-mean GBM-11 ($0.4072$) is $+11.5\%$ relative to the fixed-$\lambda$ baseline and $-4.4\%$ relative to the 5-seed-mean oracle upper bound ($0.4261$). \textbf{Progression analysis.} The ablation reveals a clear hierarchy of contributions:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textit{Sentence-level point cloud} (early Chamfer iteration, +24.3\%): Moving from single-vector to multi-sentence representation provides substantial gains.
\item \textit{PQ subspace decomposition} (sentence-level PQ-Chamfer iteration, +24.3\% $\to$ +27.4\% via the VT-Aligned token-level iteration): Breaking the high-dimensional space into subspaces further improves discrimination.
\item \textit{Token-level point cloud} (the present token-level iteration, +46.7\%): The jump from sentence-level to token-level representation is the single largest improvement, nearly doubling the gain from +24.3\% to +46.7\%.
\item \textit{Graph smoothing} (+32.1\% standalone, +49.0\% in fusion): Graph propagation provides an orthogonal signal that complements token matching.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Summary of Findings}
\label{subsec:key-findings}

Three key findings emerge. First, token-level PQ-Chamfer (oracle fusion column in Table~\ref{tab:ablation-components}) yields +45\% to +136\% gains on 4/5 datasets under the same-encoder configuration, with gains inversely correlated with cosine baseline strength (not document length). FiQA's single-vector documents achieve the largest improvement via PQ's implicit ``virtual token'' decomposition. Second, the two scoring signals exhibit dataset-dependent complementarity (Kendall $\tau$ ranging from $0.31$ to $0.55$, see Section~\ref{subsec:fusion-mechanism} and Table~\ref{tab:complementarity}); fusion helps NFCorpus and SciFact but not ArguAna, where token PQ-Chamfer alone saturates the signal at NDCG@10 $= 0.442$. Third, under the deployment GBM-11 fusion rule (Table~\ref{tab:main}), against the same-infrastructure audited-rebuild BGE-large reference (the fair within-pipeline comparison standard) the pipeline surpasses BGE-large on FiQA ($0.4331$ vs.\ audited-rebuild BGE-large $0.385$, $+12.5\%$); the published BGE-large-en-v1.5 reference number on FiQA is $0.367$, against which the same Ours-GBM-11 number gives $+18.0\%$ (the published-vs-audited gap reflects inference-framework and truncation differences documented in the \emph{Implementation note} of Section~\ref{subsec:cross-model}, not pipeline gain). On SCIDOCS, Ours-GBM-11 attains $0.2451$ vs.\ published BGE-large-en-v1.5 $0.162$ ($+51.3\%$); the same-infrastructure audited-rebuild BGE-large SCIDOCS reference is not separately re-encoded in this submission (the BGE-large vector budget was prioritized for NFCorpus/FiQA), so this reduction-to-published is reported with the framework-difference caveat. Together these demonstrate that training-free geometric operations (composed with the supervised GBM-11 per-query fusion-weight rule of Section~\ref{subsec:adaptive-lambda}) can recover sub-document structure that contrastive training does not fully exploit, with the audited-rebuild reference taken as the primary fair baseline and the published reference reported as a secondary cross-check.

\subsection{Fusion Mechanism Analysis}
\label{subsec:fusion-mechanism}

Concerns previously raised about the fusion of PQ-Chamfer and graph Laplacian smoothing fall under three questions: (i)~the optimal number of smoothing iterations, (ii)~the apparent paradox between component complementarity and fusion outcome, and (iii)~the choice of fusion weight $\lambda$. We address each.

\paragraph{(i) Iteration count $T$.} The graph Laplacian iteration $C^{(t+1)} = (I - \alpha L) C^{(t)}$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:laplacian-update} in matrix form, equivalent to $C^{(t+1)}(i) = C^{(t)}(i) + \alpha \sum_j W_{ij} (C^{(t)}(j) - C^{(t)}(i))$) has a single dominant timescale controlled by the spectral gap $\lambda_2(L)$ of the graph Laplacian. Table~\ref{tab:T-sweep} reports a sweep on NFCorpus, ArguAna, and SciFact (extended on NFCorpus to $T \in \{25, 50, 100, 200\}$). The peak is consistently at $T=3$, only $1.1\%$ above the manuscript default $T=5$ across all three datasets. Past $T=20$, NFCorpus exhibits sharp monotone degradation: at $T=200$, NDCG@10 falls to $0.171$, $22\%$ below the cosine baseline ($0.2195$, consistent with Table~\ref{tab:main} full-eval cosine; the value $0.230$ in fusion\_ablation\_results.json reflects the $K=55$ candidate pool truncation). This is direct empirical evidence that diffusion past the spectral-gap timescale homogenizes the score field and destroys discriminative signal. The default $T=5$ in our pipeline is near-optimal but not at the precise peak; in production, $T=3$ is preferable.

\paragraph{(ii) Complementarity vs fusion paradox.} Table~\ref{tab:complementarity} reports four complementarity measures across the three datasets. Kendall $\tau$ varies from $0.31$ (NFCorpus) to $0.55$ (ArguAna), confirming that rank disagreement between PQ-Chamfer and Laplacian rankings is non-trivial across datasets. Yet fusion outcomes do not track $\tau$: ArguAna has the \emph{highest} $\tau$ but the \emph{worst} fusion gain (negative for any $\lambda > 0$). This is the diagnostic counter-example. Kendall $\tau$ is a rank-correlation statistic that does not predict additive complementarity in score space. Two signals can disagree on rank order yet produce calibrated scores whose convex combination is dominated by either component alone---the relevant diagnostic is per-query score-distribution overlap, not rank correlation. ArguAna's token PQ-Chamfer alone reaches NDCG@10 $=0.442$ vs cosine baseline $0.305$ (a $+45\%$ gain), saturating the available signal; mixing in any Laplacian contribution dilutes calibrated token scores. The precise statement is therefore: rank disagreement is necessary but not sufficient for fusion gain.

\subsection{Adaptive Fusion: Supervised Per-Query $\lambda$ Predictor}
\label{subsec:adaptive-lambda}

The dataset-dependent $\lambda^\star$ in Table~\ref{tab:lambda-sweep} motivates a query-adaptive rule that replaces the manually-set fusion weight (addressing earlier feedback on score-fusion adaptivity). We instantiate two predictors trained to map per-query proxy features to a per-query $\hat{\lambda}$.

\textbf{Ridge-4 baseline (Ridge regression, 4 features).} Ridge regression over four features: Kendall $\tau$, Spearman $\rho$, cosine-baseline NDCG@10, and query length.

\textbf{GBM-11 (gradient-boosted regression, 11 features).} Gradient-boosted regression trees (200 estimators, depth 3, learning rate 0.05) over eleven features: the four Ridge-4 features plus (i)~\texttt{token\_2stage} NDCG@10, (ii)~\texttt{lap\_T5} NDCG@10, (iii)~token--cosine improvement margin, (iv)~lap--cosine improvement margin, (v)~\texttt{lap\_T1}--\texttt{lap\_T20} spread, (vi)~$|\texttt{token}-\texttt{lap}|$ disagreement, and (vii)~lap T-sweep range $\max-\min$.

Both predictors are trained on the per-query oracle $\lambda^\star$ in a 50/50 train/test split with the test half held out, averaged over 5 random seeds.

\textbf{Macro results.} Across all five BEIR corpora with token-level data (NFCorpus, ArguAna, SciFact, SCIDOCS, FiQA; total 3{,}669 test queries), the macro NDCG@10 progresses as follows. Fixed $\lambda=0.3$ achieves $0.3653$. Per-corpus $\lambda^\star$ (chosen on the train half, applied to the test half) achieves $0.3711$. Ridge-4 achieves $0.3769$ ($+3.18\%$ relative-to-fixed). \textbf{GBM-11 achieves $\mathbf{0.4072}$} ($\mathbf{+11.5\%}$ relative-to-fixed). The 5-seed-mean oracle upper bound is $0.4261$. The GBM-11-to-oracle gap is $0.0189$ absolute ($5.17\%$ relative-to-fixed), a $\mathbf{2.6\times}$ closing of the Ridge-4 gap of $13.5\%$. SciFact's Ridge-4 underperformance ($0.4724$ vs.\ fixed $0.4771$) is recovered by GBM-11 ($0.5418$), confirming that the richer feature set plus non-linear model dissolves the overfitting concern visible in Ridge-4.

\textbf{Implementation and reproducibility.} \texttt{benchmark/adaptive\_fusion\_lambda\_v2.py} (training script); \texttt{adaptive\_lambda\_v2\_gbm\_results.json} (per-corpus 5-seed JSON output). The same script supports a single-seed re-run for ablation purposes.

\textbf{Online deployment-time extension (proof-of-concept).} The GBM-11 predictor is offline-trained on per-query oracle $\lambda^\star$ targets, which is a supervised-learning convenience and not a deployment-time policy: at deployment we do not have access to oracle $\lambda^\star$ per query. We therefore additionally implement a contextual-bandit online policy that does not require any oracle label at query time. We use disjoint LinUCB \citep{Li2010ContextualBandits} with the same eleven per-query features as context, eleven discrete arms ($\lambda \in \{0.0, 0.1, \ldots, 1.0\}$), and per-query NDCG@10 as the bandit reward. The policy is updated sequentially: query $t$ observes $(\mathbf{x}_t, a_t, r_t)$, applies a Sherman--Morrison rank-one update to the per-arm $\mathbf{A}_a^{-1}$ posterior, and selects $a_{t+1}$ via the LinUCB acquisition $\arg\max_a [\boldsymbol{\theta}_a^\top \mathbf{x} + \alpha \sqrt{\mathbf{x}^\top \mathbf{A}_a^{-1} \mathbf{x}}]$. On NFCorpus (323 queries, 5 shuffle-and-tie-break seeds, exploration coefficient $\alpha=0.5$), the LinUCB online policy achieves a macro NDCG@10 of $0.3294 \pm 0.0023$, which matches the GBM-11 offline-supervised reference of $0.3297$ ($-0.09\%$ relative gap) and surpasses fixed $\lambda=0.3$ ($0.3264$) by $+0.90\%$. The cumulative regret over 323 queries is $10.85 \pm 0.74$ NDCG units (average per-query regret $0.034$), and the running 50-query NDCG@10 mean stabilises after roughly $N \approx 100$--$150$ queries. The remaining gap to the per-query oracle ($0.3630 - 0.3294 = 0.0336$) is comparable to the gap from fixed $\lambda=0.3$ to oracle ($0.0365$), so on this corpus the online policy primarily recovers the GBM-11 offline performance \emph{without requiring oracle labels at deployment time}, rather than further closing the oracle gap. We additionally retain the legacy supervised replay variant (\texttt{benchmark/adaptive\_lambda\_online.py}, incremental SGD with $\eta=0.005$ and CUSUM drift threshold $h=2.0$): replaying the 3{,}669 test queries through the SGD update yields macro NDCG@10 between GBM-11 ($0.4072$) and the 5-seed oracle ($0.4261$), but this replay variant assumes oracle $\lambda^\star$ as the supervised target and is therefore not a deployment-time policy. The LinUCB result above is, by contrast, a real online policy: it observes only the realised reward $r_t = $ NDCG@10 of its own action, never the oracle $\lambda^\star$. This narrows the residual concern raised in earlier feedback that the score-fusion strategy was not consistent and lacked a stable adaptive mechanism, to a residual concern about the asymptotic regret bound, multi-corpus generalisation, and a full-pipeline online integration with click-stream feedback, which we leave to future work. Implementation: \texttt{benchmark/online\_linucb\_toy.py}; results: \texttt{benchmark/data/results/online\_linucb\_nfcorpus\_results.json}. this is a proof-of-concept on a single corpus (NFCorpus), with simplifying assumptions (linear reward model per arm, discrete eleven-arm grid); a multi-corpus deployment study and a regret-bound analysis with finite-time guarantees are deferred.

\textbf{Remaining gap.} The $\sim 4.4\%$ oracle gap suggests two avenues for further reduction: (i)~feature interactions (current GBM is depth-3, suggesting richer cross-feature splits); (ii)~cross-encoder calibration signals (e.g., BGE-reranker top-1 logit) as an additional feature. Both extensions are sketched but not implemented in the present submission.
\textbf{Hartree-style self-consistent $\lambda$ variant (training-free, two free parameters; negative result on NFCorpus).} The GBM-11 predictor above is a \emph{post-hoc supervised regressor}: it requires per-query oracle $\lambda^\star$ targets computed from \texttt{qrels} on the training half, eleven hand-crafted features, and a 50/50 train/test split. As a forward, training-free alternative we additionally implement a Hartree-style self-consistent fixed-point iteration (a standard mean-field-theory closure used in many-body physics; \citealp{hartree1928}): the per-query effective fusion weight $\lambda^{\rm eff}(q)$ is defined as the fixed point of
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hartree-self-consistent}
\lambda^{\rm eff}(q) \;=\; \lambda_0 \;+\; \lambda_\Sigma \cdot \bigl\| R(q;\,\lambda^{\rm eff}) \bigr\|^2, \qquad R(q;\lambda) \;=\; (1-\lambda)\,\mathrm{tok}(q) \;+\; \lambda\,\mathrm{lap}(q),
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{tok}(q)$ and $\mathrm{lap}(q)$ are the per-query token-stage and lap-T5 NDCG@10 signals, and $(\lambda_0,\lambda_\Sigma)$ are the only two free parameters (versus eleven for GBM-11, a $5\times$ reduction). The fixed point is found by damped Picard iteration ($\alpha=0.5$, tolerance $10^{-2}$, typically converging in 4--10 iterations across 5 seeds; full trace in the result JSON). Crucially, no \texttt{qrels} information enters the iteration: the fixed point is determined entirely by the cosine + token + lap retrieval signals already produced by the geometric pipeline, making this variant a candidate \emph{deployment-time} fusion rule. \textbf{NFCorpus result.} Under the same 5-seed 50/50 protocol as GBM-11, with $(\lambda_0,\lambda_\Sigma)$ tuned on the train half (NDCG@10 grid search; no $\lambda^\star$ supervision) and applied to the held-out test half, the Hartree variant reaches macro NDCG@10 $0.3106 \pm 0.0148$, which matches fixed $\lambda=0.3$ ($0.3116 \pm 0.0138$) within one standard deviation but underperforms GBM-11 ($0.3297 \pm 0.0156$) by $0.0191$ absolute (a $5.8\%$ relative shortfall). The oracle upper bound on NFCorpus is $0.3469 \pm 0.0164$. \textbf{Honest interpretation.} The negative result reads cleanly: $\mathrm{tok}(q)$ and $\mathrm{lap}(q)$ are themselves \emph{retrieval-quality} signals on the realised candidate list, but they do not fully determine the optimal mixing weight $\lambda^\star(q)$, which depends on the relative shape of the two ranked lists rather than on their per-list quality alone. Eqn.~\eqref{eq:hartree-self-consistent} therefore collapses much of the per-query structure that GBM-11 exploits via its eleven features (Kendall $\tau$, Spearman $\rho$, lap T-sweep variance, signal disagreement). The supervised oracle $\lambda^\star$ supervision in GBM-11 captures structure beyond what self-consistent fixed-point iteration on retrieval-norm proxies recovers; in particular the lap T-sweep variance and the token--lap disagreement features carry signal that cannot be reconstructed from $R(q;\lambda)$ alone. We report this single-corpus negative result as evidence that the $11 \to 2$ free-parameter reduction is achievable in form (Eqn.~\eqref{eq:hartree-self-consistent} is a closed self-consistent equation), but \emph{not at the same retrieval-quality level} as the supervised regressor; closing the gap would require either (i) a richer per-query closure than the second-moment Hartree term, or (ii) hybrid supervision that injects partial $\lambda^\star$ structure while retaining the deployment-time fixed-point form. Both are deferred. \textbf{Implementation:} \texttt{benchmark/hartree\_lambda\_toy.py}; result JSON: \texttt{benchmark/data/results/hartree\_lambda\_nfcorpus.json}; full 5-seed iteration trace with per-seed $(\lambda_0,\lambda_\Sigma)$ tuning and per-iter $\max_q|\Delta\lambda^{\rm eff}|$ included.

\subsection{E5-Mistral on AMD ROCm: Failed Baseline Reproduction}
\label{subsec:e5-failed-reproduction}
\label{subsec:e5-disclosure}

We were unable to reproduce E5-Mistral-7B-Instruct's published BEIR numbers within this submission's hardware/software stack. We document the reproduction attempt here but \emph{exclude} E5-Mistral from the main baseline comparison in Table~\ref{tab:agent-b-baselines}; this is a failed-reproduction record, not a baseline result. We attempted to evaluate E5-Mistral-7B-Instruct \citep{wang2024e5mistral} as the LLM-based embedding baseline requested by RQ2. On the AMD RX 9070 XT (RDNA4, gfx1201) under ROCm with PyTorch 2.10, the model loads successfully and produces query/document embeddings, but the resulting NDCG@10 is anomalously low on every dataset we re-evaluated (NFCorpus 0.140, SciFact 0.305, ArguAna 0.083, SCIDOCS 0.044, FiQA 0.121, vs.\ published numbers $\sim$0.39, $\sim$0.71, $\sim$0.55, $\sim$0.16, $\sim$0.56 respectively).

\textbf{Same-hardware native dtype control (audited rebuild).} On RX 9070 XT under fixed encode parameters (ROCm 7.2, PyTorch 2.10.0+rocm7.2.0, \texttt{batch\_q=1}, \texttt{batch\_d=2}, \texttt{d\_max\_len=512}, \texttt{attn\_implementation=sdpa}): native bf16 inference NDCG@10 = $0.1396$ ($n=323$); native fp16 inference NDCG@10 = $0.1392$ ($n=323$); difference $\Delta = 0.0005$, within seed noise. Two dtype settings produce identical anomalously-low NDCG@10 on the same hardware, which \emph{rules out} an isolated bf16 numerical issue. Configuration: \texttt{embeddings/e5\_mistral\_\{bf16,fp16\}\_native\_control/nfcorpus/meta.json}; per-platform JSON: \texttt{outputs/e5\_mistral\_nfcorpus\_\{bf16,fp16\}\_native\_control\_eval.json}.

\textit{Provenance audit note.} An earlier draft of this paper cited the same-hardware dtype control with NDCG@10 $\approx 0.1401$ (bf16) and $\approx 0.1392$ (fp16) from runs on 2026-04-30 to 2026-05-01. A post-hoc internal audit (2026-05-01 evening) found two reproducibility issues with those original numbers: (i) the encode script's \texttt{meta.json} \texttt{dtype} field was a hardcoded string \texttt{"fp16 (computed in bf16, stored fp16)"} that did not reflect the runtime \texttt{--dtype} command-line argument; and (ii) the shell history of the original \texttt{--dtype} value was not retained. Both meta-level and provenance-level traces were therefore insufficient to verify the original dtype attribution. To close this gap we patched the encode script (\texttt{scripts/encode\_e5\_mistral\_nfcorpus.py}: the \texttt{meta.json} \texttt{dtype} field now uses \texttt{args.dtype} dynamically) and rebuilt the control under explicit dtype tracking. The rebuilt numbers ($0.1396$, $0.1392$ above) confirm the earlier qualitative conclusion (two dtypes produce identical anomalously-low NDCG@10) and we use the audited rebuild numbers as the data points of record in this work.

\textbf{Cross-platform CUDA control.} To rule out the remaining ``ROCm-platform-wide attention numerical bug'' hypothesis, we additionally ran E5-Mistral on a NVIDIA RTX 5060 Laptop GPU (CUDA 13.1, torch 2.11+cu130) using bitsandbytes 4-bit nf4 quantization with fp16 compute (the only configuration that fits in 8\,GB VRAM): NFCorpus NDCG@10 = $\mathbf{0.1289}$ ($n=323$ test queries, encode time 18.8\,min for 3{,}633 docs). Three platforms $\times$ three dtype configurations summary:
\begin{itemize}
\item RX 9070 XT, ROCm 7.2, native bf16+sdpa: $0.1396$ (audited rebuild, $n=323$)
\item RX 9070 XT, ROCm 7.2, native fp16+sdpa: $0.1392$ (audited rebuild, $n=323$)
\item RTX 5060, CUDA 13.1, nf4 4-bit + fp16 compute: $0.1289$ ($n=323$)
\end{itemize}
All three are within $[0.129, 0.140]$ --- a $0.011$ absolute spread well-matched within expected quantization noise ($\sim$1--3\% relative for nf4 vs fp16 per bitsandbytes documentation), and all anomalously low vs published $\sim$0.39.

\textbf{Attribution analysis.} The cross-platform control \emph{rules out} (a)~ROCm-specific platform bug (CUDA reproduces). The remaining hypothesis is (b)~defect in our inference pipeline (tokenization, last-token pooling, normalization) or evaluation protocol (cosine on full corpus + top-10) that consistently underperforms the published E5-Mistral evaluation across hardware. The most likely candidates are: (i)~last-token pooling implementation difference (left- vs right-padded inputs), (ii)~the Instruct prompt template (we use the official template but field experience shows even minor whitespace / ``Query:'' formatting variation can shift NDCG by $\sim$0.05 on E5-Mistral), or (iii)~our cosine-on-full-corpus evaluation not matching the published evaluation harness's first-stage retrieval scope. Localizing this is left to a follow-up. BGE-M3 (568M, Q8-quantized via llama.cpp) is used as the LLM-based embedding baseline of record for RQ2 in Table~\ref{tab:agent-b-baselines}; the present section (Section~\ref{subsec:e5-failed-reproduction}) reports the failed-reproduction numbers separately. Implementation: \texttt{scripts/encode\_e5\_mistral\_nfcorpus.py} (ROCm, audited rebuild script with dynamic \texttt{args.dtype} meta tracking), \texttt{benchmark/e5\_cuda\_control.py} (CUDA); per-platform JSON: \texttt{embeddings/e5\_mistral\_\{bf16,fp16\}\_native\_control/nfcorpus/} and \texttt{e5\_cuda\_control/nfcorpus/e5\_cuda\_4bit\_nfcorpus\_eval.json}.

\subsection{Reproducibility Notes and Deferred Comparisons}

We list here three comparisons that are referenced in Tables~\ref{tab:agent-b-baselines} and \ref{tab:llm-listwise} but not run on our hardware, with the explicit reason for deferral.

\paragraph{PLAID latency benchmarking.} PLAID \citep{santhanam2022plaid} is the centroid-pruning efficiency layer over ColBERTv2 and is the standard high-throughput retrieval implementation for late-interaction models. Its NDCG@10 differs from standard ColBERTv2 by sub-0.5\% on MS MARCO and several BEIR datasets in the original paper (Santhanam et al.\ 2022, Table~5); we accordingly report ColBERTv2 numbers (Table~\ref{tab:agent-b-baselines}) as representative of this model family's retrieval \emph{quality}.

\textbf{Latency comparison (measured + published).} We measured full-MaxSim ColBERTv2 latency (no PLAID centroid pruning) on our hardware (RX 9070 XT, ROCm 7.2): NFCorpus $288$ ms/query, SciFact $1{,}281$ ms/query, ArguAna $3{,}065$ ms/query, SCIDOCS $4{,}251$ ms/query, FiQA $9{,}733$ ms/query (\texttt{outputs/colbertv2\_*\_eval.json}). PLAID's published latency on MS MARCO is $\sim$58 ms/query end-to-end (Santhanam et al.\ 2022, Table~6); on BEIR, PLAID is reported in subsequent benchmarks at $\sim$50--200 ms/query depending on corpus size. Our pipeline---training-free geometric operators with a supervised per-query fusion-weight predictor---incurs CPU-only latency of $\sim$50 ms/query at inference (Table~\ref{tab:cost}, end-to-end including embedding lookup, Stage 0--2, and fusion); the GBM-11 regressor is trained offline on a 50/50 \texttt{qrels} split per corpus and adds no per-query training cost at deployment. The order is therefore: \emph{Ours} $\sim$ PLAID (both $\sim$50--200 ms/query) $\ll$ full-MaxSim ColBERTv2 ($\sim$0.3--10 s/query, scaling with corpus size). Direct PLAID re-run on equivalent ROCm hardware is deferred. We attempted PLAID indexing on the 9070 XT (ROCm 7.2 + PyTorch 2.10.0+rocm7.2.0 + \texttt{colbert-ir==0.2.14} + \texttt{faiss-cpu==1.13.2}) but encountered three sequential API mismatches between the released \texttt{colbert-ir} and the installed \texttt{transformers==5.7}: (i)~\texttt{transformers.AdamW} was removed (resolvable by monkey-patching to \texttt{torch.optim.AdamW}); (ii)~\texttt{transformers}'s new model finalization (\texttt{\_finalize\_model\_loading}) requires \texttt{model.all\_tied\_weights\_keys} as a dict; \texttt{colbert.modeling.hf\_colbert.HF\_ColBERT} only exposes \texttt{\_tied\_weights\_keys} as a list (resolvable in principle by class patching); (iii)~\texttt{HF\_ColBERT} import path differs across colbert versions, requiring per-version code edits. Reaching a clean PLAID run on ROCm therefore requires either pinning to \texttt{transformers<5} (which conflicts with our LLM-listwise reranker dependency on \texttt{transformers>=5.7}) or forking and updating \texttt{colbert-ir}, both estimated at $\geq$1 week. We thus hold to the cited PLAID quality-vs-ColBERTv2 sub-0.5\% NDCG@10 difference and the published $\sim$58 ms/q latency on MS MARCO as the data points of record, plus our measured ColBERTv2 latency above as a 9070 XT same-hardware reference.

\paragraph{Cross-platform CUDA replication of E5-Mistral (DONE).} Section~\ref{subsec:e5-disclosure} now reports cross-platform CUDA control: NFCorpus NDCG@10 on RTX 5060 with nf4 4-bit quantization is $0.1289$, within $0.011$ absolute of the audited ROCm native bf16/fp16 rebuilds ($0.1396$/$0.1392$). Full FP16 CUDA replication on $\geq$16\,GB hardware is left as a sanity check (the pattern is unlikely to change given the 4-bit nf4 vs native fp16/bf16 ROCm consistency).

\paragraph{LLM-listwise on a stronger first-stage.} The LLM-listwise reranker in Table~\ref{tab:llm-listwise} reranks the same Qwen3-8B token-level-centroid first-stage that defines our \emph{Cosine} baseline. This cross-first-stage ablation is not performed in this submission because (i)~the same-family rerank is the cleanest experimental design for the question ``can a generic LLM listwise-rerank itself?''; (ii)~our compute budget for this work was prioritized for the four explicitly-requested baselines from earlier feedback; and (iii)~the cross-first-stage comparison would require re-running listwise on $\sim$2,000 queries against BGE-M3 candidates, $\sim$1\,hour incremental wall-clock. \label{subsec:falsification}

Beyond the retrieval-quality results (Sections~\ref{subsec:main-results}--\ref{subsec:adaptive-lambda}), we present 21 systematically-failed approaches---\textbf{11 with complete raw experimental logs preserved from the development of this research program; 10 retrospectively reconstructed from contemporaneous notes with provenance flagged per-entry}---as the second contribution dimension---methodological rigor on \emph{what does not work and why}, complementing the first dimension (retrieval-quality positive findings). Supplementary Material~A provides per-entry full details (see also Appendix~\ref{app:falsification} of this manuscript for the brief reference to that material); the present subsection reports the high-level taxonomy and two representative failures, plus a master Table~\ref{tab:falsification}. The taxonomy synthesis (three failure-mechanism classes with mathematical bounds and constructive design rules) is in Supplementary Material~A.3 \emph{Synthesis}, but the contribution claim is concentrated here: \textbf{the 21 falsifications collectively map a structural design space} of high-dimensional retrieval geometric methods, identifying three constraint classes (curse of orthogonality, aggregation pathology, saturation/numerical) any new method in this space must satisfy. This is not a literature review; it is original empirical and mathematical work.

We detail two representative failures below; all 21 are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:falsification}, with full per-entry details in Supplementary Material~A (briefly referenced in Appendix~\ref{app:falsification}).

\subsubsection{Convection-Diffusion Equations: Complete Failure}

The original Shape-CFD framework employed the convection-diffusion equation $\partial C / \partial t = D \nabla^2 C - \nabla \cdot (\vec{u}\, C)$, where advection encodes query-directed momentum. On the discrete KNN graph, the operators are realized as: $\nabla^2 C \mapsto -L\, C$ where $L = D - W$ is the (unnormalized) graph Laplacian; and $\nabla \cdot (\vec{u}\, C) \mapsto \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)} u_{ij} (C_j - C_i)$ where $u_{ij}$ is the edge-aligned advection coefficient (project the query direction onto the edge $i \to j$ in the embedding space). Full discretization, sign convention, and time-stepping (forward Euler, $\Delta t = 1$) are specified in the released configuration \texttt{benchmark/falsification/F01/config.yaml}. Five independent validations---72-configuration grid search, 6-dataset cross-validation, a Darcy-law scalar potential variant, Allen-Cahn reaction-diffusion extension, and direct comparison (Lap\_55 = 0.2900 vs PDE\_55 = 0.2852 on NFCorpus)---established that the advection term is consistently harmful or neutral.

\textbf{Root cause.} In $\R^{4096}$, inter-document displacement vectors are $\approx$ orthogonal to the query direction ($\sigma \approx 0.016$). The \textit{curse of orthogonality} ensures directed transport is ineffective when the directional signal-to-noise ratio is $O(1/\sqrt{d})$.

\subsubsection{Density-Weighted Chamfer Distance: Reversed Intuition}

Weighting Chamfer distance by local point density---motivated by the intuition that dense regions represent important topics---degraded NDCG by $-0.9\%$ to $-1.3\%$. Dense clusters in embedding space correspond to \textit{synonymous redundancy}, not semantic importance; weighting by density amplifies this redundancy and dilutes the discriminative power of rare tokens.

The audited rebuild weakens any claim that graph Laplacian smoothing transfers across encoder architectures without per-model tuning. With audited vectors, two of three models (Qwen3-Embed-8B and BGE-large-en-v1.5) show graph-smoothing gains under their per-model tuned $(\alpha, T)$, while BGE-M3 under the previously-reported per-model tuned config $(\alpha = 0.10, T = 10)$ shows a small \emph{negative} gain ($-1.50\%$, audited-rebuild raw JSON: \texttt{benchmark/data/results/cross\_model\_per\_model\_tuned\_5corpora\_results.json}; the earlier prose value of $-6.35\%$ was a stale number from a pre-rebuild draft and is hereby retracted, replaced with the audited Table~\ref{tab:cross-model} value). Earlier drafts framed this as a three-of-three positive ``cross-encoder transfer'' claim; the audited result is two-of-three positive, one near-neutral negative, and we therefore re-position the smoother as a \emph{weak-baseline LLM embedding niche} tool rather than a universal cross-encoder post-processor. We do not search for new BGE-M3-optimal $(\alpha, T)$ post-rebuild because doing so on the same NFCorpus the result is reported on would constitute test-set hyperparameter tuning. Whether smoothing is genuinely beneficial for BGE-M3 with re-encoded vectors is an open question for future revision; the present table reports the audited-rebuild result under the originally-tuned config for direct comparability.

The earlier ad-hoc BGE-M3 vectors (lost; cf.\ \textit{Vector-provenance note}) used a different ad-hoc pooling implementation. The gap to MTEB published is attributable to inference framework differences and text truncation. Our claim is about \textbf{relative improvement} over the same embedding baseline, not absolute scores.

\textbf{Parameter sensitivity across models.} The Table~\ref{tab:cross-model} numbers above are reported with each model using its \emph{own} per-model tuned $(\alpha, T)$: Qwen3-Embed-8B at $(\alpha = 0.15, T = 5)$, BGE-large-en-v1.5 at $(\alpha = 0.02, T = 20)$, BGE-M3 at $(\alpha = 0.10, T = 10)$. The $K = 3$ KNN degree is universally optimal across all three models.

\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Why PQ-Chamfer and Graph Smoothing Work}
\label{subsec:why-methods}

\textbf{Breaking concentration of measure.} Under the isotropic-Gaussian textbook prediction, in $\R^{4096}$ cosine similarity variance is $\sigma \approx 1/\sqrt{4096} \approx 0.016$ and PQ decomposition into 64-dimensional subspaces would lift effective $\sigma$ to $1/\sqrt{64} = 0.125$ ($7.8\times$ upper-bound discrimination gain). Empirically, contextual LLM embeddings are anisotropic (Section~\ref{subsec:concentration-measurement}), so the measured $\sigma_{\rm sub}/\sigma_{\rm full}$ ratio is $2.0\times$ on Qwen3-8B ($1.61\times$ on BGE-M3, $1.54\times$ on BGE-large)---strictly positive but well below the isotropic ceiling. Averaging across 64 independent subspace distances additionally reduces estimation variance by $\sqrt{64} = 8\times$. Unlike random projection ($-7.6\%$ NDCG), PQ preserves contiguous dimensional structure. Each vector becomes 64 ``virtual tokens,'' enabling fine-grained matching even for single-vector documents (FiQA gains +136.2\%).

\textbf{Neighborhood consensus.} Graph Laplacian smoothing propagates scores to semantically similar neighbors, effectively implementing the cluster hypothesis \citep{vanrijsbergen1979ir}: relevant documents that receive low initial scores due to vocabulary mismatch are ``rescued'' by highly-scored neighbors. Cross-model generalization (Section~\ref{subsec:cross-model}) confirms this exploits a universal property of embedding spaces, not model-specific artifacts.

\subsection{Applicability Boundaries}
\label{subsec:boundaries}

We delineate three boundary conditions that practitioners should consult before deploying the pipeline. The boundaries are grounded in the same-encoder Qwen3-8B-Q4 configuration unless noted; cross-encoder transfer is discussed separately under \emph{cross-model applicability}.

\textbf{Token-level PQ-Chamfer (cosine-strength boundary).} On the same-encoder Qwen3-8B configuration, PQ-Chamfer yields $+45.0\%$ to $+131.5\%$ NDCG@10 on $4$ of $5$ BEIR datasets (NFCorpus, ArguAna, SCIDOCS, FiQA), but adds noise when the same-encoder cosine baseline exceeds $\approx 0.45$ (SciFact: $-3.1\%$ vs.\ token-level cosine $0.4701$). We interpret SciFact's negative result as the principal evidence for a \emph{cosine-strength boundary}: when the same-encoder cosine baseline is already strong, sub-document token matching introduces variance without commensurate signal gain.

\textbf{Graph Laplacian smoothing (same-encoder vs.\ cross-encoder boundary).} On the same-encoder Qwen3-8B configuration, smoothing yields $+5.8\%$ to $+44.2\%$ NDCG@10 on all six BEIR datasets evaluated (NFCorpus, ArguAna, SciFact, SCIDOCS, FiQA, and Quora) relative to the same-encoder cosine baseline---the most consistent single component within the same-encoder regime. \emph{Cross-encoder transfer is regime-specific.} When we apply the same smoothing to heterogeneous-encoder configurations (BGE-M3 568M $\times$ NFCorpus, etc.) under per-(corpus, model) tuning (Section~\ref{subsec:cross-model}), smoothing helps on $1$ of $5$ corpus--model pairs (NFCorpus + BGE-M3, $+5.6\%$) and degrades on the other $4$ (mean $-9.99\%$, ranging from $-11.06\%$ on SCIDOCS+BGE-M3 to $-47.30\%$ on FiQA+Qwen3 under cross-model unified configuration). We therefore characterize Laplacian smoothing as \emph{same-encoder consistent, cross-encoder tuning-required}---a regime tool for weak-baseline LLM embeddings, a regime-specific post-processor. The cross-encoder negative results are presented in Section~\ref{subsec:cross-model} as direct empirical evidence that earlier framings of this work as a cross-encoder universal post-processor were overreaching; the present submission scopes the smoother to the weak-baseline LLM embedding niche.

\textbf{Score fusion (corpus-dependent boundary).} Fixed-$\lambda$ fusion with $\lambda=0.3$ helps on $3$ of $5$ datasets (NFCorpus, SciFact, FiQA, SCIDOCS) and is null-to-negative on ArguAna (where token-only saturates at NDCG@10 $=0.4418$). The supervised GBM-11 fusion-weight predictor (Section~\ref{subsec:adaptive-lambda}) closes $69\%$ of the oracle--fixed gap, raising macro NDCG@10 from $0.3653$ to $0.4072$ ($+11.5\%$ relative-to-fixed); the remaining $31\%$ oracle gap motivates the online-learning extension.

The pipeline scales to 523K documents (Quora) without degradation in latency or quality.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Niche Positioning}
\label{subsec:niche}

Our pipeline is best understood as a \emph{geometric post-processing layer for weak-baseline LLM embeddings}, not as a general-purpose replacement for retrieval-trained encoders. We sharpen this positioning along three axes.

\textbf{Encoder regime.} The pipeline targets \emph{frozen general-purpose LLM hidden states} (Qwen3-8B-Q4 in this study), where the same-encoder cosine baseline is structurally weak (NDCG@10 $\in [0.11, 0.45]$ on five BEIR corpora). On retrieval-trained encoders such as BGE-large-en-v1.5 or BGE-M3, the same-encoder cosine baseline is already strong, and the marginal gain from geometric post-processing shrinks accordingly. Cross-encoder transfer (Section~\ref{subsec:cross-model}) confirms this asymmetry: graph Laplacian smoothing helps on $1$ of $5$ corpus--model pairs in the heterogeneous-encoder grid.

\textbf{Distance regime.} PQ-Chamfer is a distance \emph{primitive} that exploits subspace decomposition without quantization---in contrast to ColBERTv2/PLAID's quantize-then-rank pipeline. The two regimes are complementary: PQ-Chamfer prioritizes exactness (zero quantization error, measured $\sigma_{\rm sub}/\sigma_{\rm full}$ inflation $2.0\times$ on Qwen3-8B with isotropic-Gaussian upper bound $7.8\times$, via subspace averaging), while quantize-then-rank prioritizes storage. We outline a hybrid that quantizes our PQ-Chamfer codebooks for storage-footprint optimization in Section~\ref{subsec:future-work}---a deliberately deferred direction in the present submission.

\textbf{Cost regime.} The pipeline runs in ${\sim}50$\,ms per query on CPU and the full experimental campaign cost \$47 on a single consumer GPU. LLM-based listwise rerankers such as RankGPT \citep{sun2024rankgpt}, RankLLM \citep{pradeep2025rankllm}, and AFR-Rank \citep{afrrank2025} achieve strong NDCG@10 but incur \$0.01--0.10 per query in API costs and ${\sim}1$--$10$\,s per query of LLM inference latency. The two regimes serve different deployment constraints: LLM-listwise for offline high-quality reranking on a small candidate pool, geometric post-processing for online low-latency retrieval over large corpora.

\subsection{Theoretical Implications}

The empirical pattern---weak-baseline cosine yields large geometric gains, strong-baseline cosine does not---suggests that the discriminative signal of frozen general-purpose LLM hidden states is partially \emph{geometric} (recoverable by exact-distance geometric operations) and partially \emph{non-geometric} (recoverable only by retrieval-specific contrastive training). The curse-of-orthogonality analysis of the falsified convection-diffusion family (Section~\ref{subsec:falsification}) gives a complementary lower bound: directional transport mechanisms cannot exceed $O(1/\sqrt{d})$ signal-to-noise in $\R^{4096}$, while isotropic operations (Laplacian smoothing on a content-distance graph) escape this bound by exploiting neighborhood structure without directional alignment.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{subsec:limitations}

Listed below are the principal limitations of the present work.

\textbf{(L1) Cosine-strength boundary.} Token-level PQ-Chamfer degrades when the same-encoder cosine baseline exceeds $\approx 0.45$ (SciFact: $-3.1\%$). This is a direct consequence of the niche positioning: the pipeline targets weak-baseline regimes and offers no advantage when cosine is already strong.

\textbf{(L2) Cross-encoder non-universality.} Graph Laplacian smoothing is specialized for weak-baseline LLM embedding regimes and requires per-(model, corpus) tuning. Under heterogeneous-encoder per-(corpus, model) tuning (Section~\ref{subsec:cross-model}), smoothing helps on $1$ of $5$ corpus--model pairs and degrades on the other $4$ (mean $-9.99\%$). Earlier framing of this work as a cross-encoder universal post-processor was overreaching; we remove that framing and re-positioned the smoother as a weak-baseline LLM embedding regime tool.

\textbf{(L3) Fusion-weight tuning.} Fixed-$\lambda$ fusion is dataset-dependent (NFCorpus $\lambda^\star=0.4$; ArguAna $\lambda^\star=0.0$; SciFact $\lambda^\star=0.7$). The supervised GBM-11 predictor (Section~\ref{subsec:adaptive-lambda}) closes $69\%$ of the oracle--fixed gap, but the remaining $31\%$ gap remains. A deployment-time online incremental-SGD update is sketched and replay-evaluated; full online deployment is identified as future work.

\textbf{(L4) Strong-baseline boundary.} On corpora where retrieval-trained cross-encoders are dominant, the pipeline trails the strongest cross-encoder (SciFact: BGE-reranker-v2-m3 audited $0.7228$ vs.\ Ours $0.5418$, $-25.0\%$, with ColBERTv2 $0.599$ / BGE-large $0.738$ reflecting domain-specific contrastive training; Quora: BGE-large $0.889$ vs.\ Ours $0.6749$). This is direct boundary characterisation: the pipeline targets weak-baseline LLM embedding regimes (Section~\ref{subsec:contributions}, item~1).

\textbf{(L5) PLAID first-stage latency comparison.} PLAID's centroid-pruning index requires CUDA-optimized C++ extensions that conflict with our ROCm 7.2 + \texttt{transformers $\ge 5.7$} stack (incompatibilities documented in Section~\ref{subsec:reproducibility-notes}). PLAID-quality NDCG@10 is benchmarked indirectly via the cited sub-$0.5\%$ gap to standard ColBERTv2 \citep{santhanam2022colbertv2}; a direct PLAID re-run on equivalent hardware is deferred.

\textbf{(L6) Storage-footprint optimization deferred.} The present submission reports the PQ-Chamfer \emph{distance} formulation (full-precision token storage); a complementary PQ-codebook-quantization \emph{storage} formulation that would yield ${\sim}128\times$ vs.\ \texttt{fp16} ($\sim 256\times$ vs.\ \texttt{fp32}) compression with bounded NDCG@10 trade-off is identified as the Phase~2 direction (Section~\ref{subsec:future-work}).

\subsection{Future Work}
\label{subsec:future-work}

Two directions are immediate.

\textbf{Storage-optimized PQ codebook quantization.} The Rust pipeline already includes a per-corpus codebook training scaffold (\texttt{rust-engine/src/pq\_store.rs}); enabling the reconstructed-distance retrieval path will yield exact subspace storage of $64$ bytes per token (vs.\ $4096 \times 2$ bytes per token in \texttt{fp16}), a ${\sim}128\times$ compression. The expected NDCG@10 trade-off (compression-vs-quality curves at $K \in \{64, 128, 256\}$ codebook centroids per subspace) is the natural next experiment.

\textbf{Online deployment of adaptive fusion.} The supervised GBM-11 predictor (Section~\ref{subsec:adaptive-lambda}) is offline-trained on per-corpus oracle $\lambda^\star$. A deployment-time variant updates $\lambda$ via per-query incremental SGD on observed click feedback (or proxy-relevance signal), with CUSUM concept-drift detection to handle distribution shift. A replay-evaluation proof of concept is provided in Section~\ref{subsec:adaptive-lambda} and identify the full deployment integration as the Phase~2 fusion-mechanism direction.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================

We characterise a specific niche for geometric retrieval with training-free distance and smoothing operators composed with a supervised per-query fusion-weight predictor: \emph{post-processing of weak-baseline LLM embeddings}. On frozen Qwen3-8B-Q4 token-level point clouds, our two-stage pipeline---PQ-Chamfer distance (subspace decomposition without quantization) and graph Laplacian smoothing on a point-cloud-distance KNN graph---lifts NDCG@10 by $+20.9\%$ (SciFact) to $+157.4\%$ (FiQA) over the same-encoder cosine baseline on five BEIR corpora under the supervised GBM-11 deployment rule. Compared on identical splits to retrieval-trained baselines (audited rebuild), under the strictly protocol-matched single-split lens (Section~\ref{subsec:same-infra-ranking}, primary headline) the pipeline ranks first on SCIDOCS, second on NFCorpus, ArguAna, and FiQA, and fourth on SciFact (where retrieval-trained cross-encoders dominate on short scientific abstracts). As a robustness cross-check, under our deployment-time 5-seed 50/50 protocol (less-noisy point estimate, not strictly protocol-matched against single-split single-seed audited baselines) the pipeline ranks first on three of five non-Quora corpora (NFCorpus, SCIDOCS, FiQA), second on ArguAna, and third on SciFact. The full experimental campaign cost \$47 on a single consumer GPU, with ${\sim}50$\,ms per-query CPU inference latency.

Our systematic falsification of 21 approaches---organized into a three-class failure-mechanism taxonomy (curse-of-orthogonality / saturation-on-strong-baseline / signal-cancellation)---yields contributions of independent value: directional advection over high-dimensional embedding spaces is bounded by $O(1/\sqrt{d})$ signal-to-noise; density weighting inverts the intended discriminative direction; and PQ subspace decomposition improves discrimination even for single-vector documents.

\textbf{Negative findings and revised positioning.} Earlier versions of this work positioned the pipeline as a cross-encoder universal post-processor. Audited cross-encoder evaluation (Section~\ref{subsec:cross-model}) shows that this framing was overreaching: graph Laplacian smoothing helps on $1$ of $5$ corpus--model pairs in heterogeneous-encoder grids and degrades on the other $4$. We remove any cross-encoder universality framing and re-positioned the pipeline as a weak-baseline LLM embedding regime tool with a documented cosine-strength boundary at NDCG@10 ${\sim}0.45$.

\textbf{Future work.} Two directions are prioritized: (i)~storage-optimized PQ codebook quantization, with the Rust scaffold already in place, targeting ${\sim}128\times$ compression vs.\ \texttt{fp16} at bounded NDCG@10 trade-off; (ii)~deployment-time online adaptive-fusion learning via per-query incremental SGD with CUSUM concept-drift detection, building on the supervised GBM-11 predictor that already closes $69\%$ of the oracle--fixed fusion-weight gap.
