\begin{abstract}
We propose a \textbf{training-free geometric reranking pipeline} that improves dense retrieval without any task-specific training. Documents are represented as token-level point clouds extracted from a general-purpose LLM, then scored via two complementary mechanisms: (1)~\textbf{PQ-Chamfer distance}, which decomposes embeddings into 64 subspaces of 64 dimensions each, breaking the concentration-of-measure effect; and (2)~\textbf{graph Laplacian smoothing}, which propagates relevance scores over a KNN document graph. On \textbf{six BEIR datasets} spanning 2,473 to 522,931 documents, the pipeline achieves NDCG@10 gains of \textbf{+6.0\% to +136.2\%} over cosine baselines, surpassing \textbf{BGE-large-en-v1.5} on two benchmarks (FiQA: 0.398 vs 0.367; SCIDOCS: 0.218 vs 0.162). Improvements are statistically significant ($p < 0.05$) across \textbf{three embedding models} (8B, 568M, 335M parameters), with gains inversely proportional to baseline quality. The entire experimental pipeline costs \textbf{\$47} on a single consumer GPU. We document \textbf{21 falsified approaches} (11 with complete raw experimental logs, 10 retrospectively reconstructed from V1--V13 development notes; provenance details in Appendix~A.3)---including the complete failure of convection-diffusion equations---establishing clear applicability boundaries. Code, indices, and falsification records will be released upon acceptance at an anonymized URL.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
% ============================================================

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) has become the dominant paradigm for grounding large language models in external knowledge \citep{lewis2020rag,guu2020realm}. The standard RAG pipeline employs a bi-encoder to map queries and documents into a shared embedding space, then ranks candidates by cosine similarity.  Let $\mathbf{v}_q \in \R^d$ and $\mathbf{v}_i \in \R^d$ denote the query and document embeddings, respectively:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cosine}
\text{score}(d_i) = \frac{\mathbf{v}_q \cdot \mathbf{v}_i}{\|\mathbf{v}_q\| \, \|\mathbf{v}_i\|}
\end{equation}

This formulation has two fundamental limitations. First, it represents each document as a \textit{single point} in $\R^d$, conflating diverse semantic facets into one centroid. Second, it scores each document independently, ignoring inter-document structure---topic clusters, semantic neighborhoods, and distributional patterns---that the retrieved set collectively encodes.

Existing training-free remedies do not address these two limitations directly. Query-rewriting methods such as HyDE~\citep{gao2023hyde} and Query2Doc~\citep{wang2023query2doc} alter the query but leave the document representation intact, so cosine concentration in $\R^d$ persists at scoring time. Sparse--dense fusion with BM25~\citep{robertson2009bm25} adds lexical signal but does not recover sub-document structure, and shares cosine's pointwise scoring assumption on the dense side. Pseudo-relevance feedback (PRF) over dense embeddings~\citep{li2023ance-prf} expands the query in embedding space, again inheriting concentration of measure. Late-interaction architectures such as ColBERT and ColBERTv2~\citep{khattab2020colbert,santhanam2022colbertv2} explicitly preserve token-level structure but require dedicated training of a token-aware encoder, contradicting the training-free regime. The gap we identify, therefore, is the absence of a method that (a)~exploits the token-level geometry of frozen general-purpose embeddings and (b)~scores documents jointly through inter-document structure---without any retrieval-specific training.

We address this gap by treating documents as point clouds rather than points \citep{chen2026a}. The per-token hidden states of a language model form a cloud in embedding space whose spread, density variation, and subspace projections carry semantic information that a single centroid discards. Two design questions follow: what distance metric is appropriate between document point clouds, and how can inter-document structure be incorporated without the cost of cross-encoder inference? We answer both with training-free geometric operations: PQ-Chamfer distance for the first, graph Laplacian smoothing for the second.

\textbf{Relation to prior versions.} This paper extends the Shape-CFD research program \citep{chen2026a,chen2026b}. Earlier versions (V1--V4) explored convection-diffusion equations and sentence-level point clouds. The present work (V5/V11) abandons the convection-diffusion narrative---experimentally falsified across five independent validations (Section~\ref{subsec:falsification})---and reorganizes the framework around token-level point clouds, PQ-Chamfer distance, and graph Laplacian smoothing.

\subsection{Contributions}
\label{subsec:contributions}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Training-free geometric reranking paradigm.} We show that geometric operations on general-purpose LLM hidden states can surpass single-vector dense retrieval baselines (BGE-large-en-v1.5, BGE-M3) on FiQA and SCIDOCS, without any retrieval-specific training. Our method does \emph{not} match purpose-trained cross-encoder rerankers (BGE-reranker-v2-m3) on every dataset---specifically on ArguAna (0.4418 vs.\ BGE-reranker 0.4820, $-8.3\%$) and SciFact (0.4946 vs.\ ColBERTv2 0.6141, $-19.4\%$); see Table~\ref{tab:agent-b-baselines}. The contribution is the demonstration that a generic LLM's geometric structure can compete with single-vector trained retrievers, not that it dominates all trained reranker families.

\item \textbf{PQ-Chamfer distance metric.} We repurpose product quantization from compression to distance computation\footnote{Chamfer distance is a semimetric; we use ``distance'' following point cloud convention.}, decomposing the embedding space into 64 contiguous 64-dimensional subspaces to break the concentration-of-measure effect.

\item \textbf{Token-level point cloud retrieval.} Per-token hidden states from Qwen3-8B (\texttt{--pooling none}) form document point clouds (${\sim}$356 tokens), enabling fine-grained token-to-token matching without training.

\item \textbf{Graph Laplacian regularization.} KNN graph smoothing yields consistent improvements on all six BEIR datasets (+7.7\% to +44.2\% vs cosine), making it the most robust single component.

\item \textbf{Model-agnostic post-processing.} Statistically significant gains across three embedding models (8B, 568M, 335M parameters) spanning 1024d--4096d confirm that the geometric operations exploit universal structural properties.

\item \textbf{Systematic falsification.} We document 21 failed approaches---11 with complete raw experimental logs (per-query NDCG@10, configuration files, notes) preserved from V1--V13 development, plus 10 retrospectively reconstructed from contemporaneous notes and aggregate result summaries (provenance flagged per-entry; full disclosure in Appendix~A.3). The 21 entries together establish clear applicability boundaries.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Research Objectives}
\label{subsec:research-objectives}

This study addresses the following research questions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{RQ1:} Can training-free geometric operations on general-purpose LLM hidden states improve dense retrieval quality?
\item \textbf{RQ2:} Is the improvement model-agnostic, generalizing across different embedding architectures?
\item \textbf{RQ3:} At what computational cost can these improvements be achieved compared to training specialized retrieval models?
\end{enumerate}

\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================

\textbf{Late-interaction multi-vector retrieval.} ColBERT~\citep{khattab2020colbert} introduced token-level late interaction with the asymmetric MaxSim operator $\mathrm{MaxSim}(q, d) = \sum_{i \in q} \max_{j \in d} \langle q_i, d_j\rangle$. ColBERTv2~\citep{santhanam2022colbertv2} compresses token embeddings via residual quantization with codebook training, and PLAID~\citep{santhanam2022plaid} accelerates search through centroid-based candidate generation. Our work differs from this family on three structural axes: (i)~\emph{representation source}: we use frozen general-purpose LLM hidden states (Qwen3, BGE, GTE) without retrieval-specific training, whereas ColBERT family encoders are explicitly trained on query--document pairs; (ii)~\emph{aggregation symmetry}: PQ-Chamfer is symmetric and operates on subspace cosine distances averaged across $M=64$ subspaces ($d_{\mathrm{PQ}} = \frac{1}{M} \sum_s \delta_s(q^{(s)}, d^{(s)})$ with $\delta_s$ the cosine distance in subspace $s$, see Section~\ref{sec:method}), while MaxSim is asymmetric, dot-product based, and operates on full-dimensional vectors; (iii)~\emph{cross-document signal}: we introduce an inter-document graph Laplacian step that no late-interaction method performs --- ColBERT family scores each document independently, whereas our pipeline additionally propagates relevance across a document KNN graph. Recent LLM-based embeddings such as E5-Mistral~\citep{wang2024e5mistral} and GTE~\citep{li2024gte} produce strong dense representations but still require contrastive fine-tuning; we treat any such encoder as a frozen feature extractor and apply geometric reranking on top of it.

\textbf{Two structural novelties.} Two design choices are, to our knowledge, novel in this combination. First, we use product quantization \emph{without quantization} as a distance: the subspace decomposition $\R^{4096} = \bigoplus_{s=1}^{64} \R^{64}$ is exploited to compute exact cosine distances within each subspace and then average, rather than to store and search over learned codebooks. This breaks concentration of measure (Section~\ref{sec:method}) without introducing quantization error. Second, we apply a graph Laplacian smoothing step over a $k$-NN graph built from \emph{point-cloud distances} (PQ-Chamfer between document token clouds), not from centroid cosine. This gives the smoothing operator a graph topology that respects sub-document structure, in contrast to standard graph reranking methods such as G-RAG~\citep{glass2024grag} or GAR~\citep{macavaney2022gar} that build graphs from single-vector similarities.

\textbf{LLM-based reranking.} RankGPT \citep{sun2024rankgpt} demonstrates that LLMs can serve as effective listwise rerankers via prompt engineering. RankLLM \citep{pradeep2025rankllm} provides a scalable framework for LLM-based reranking. AFR-Rank \citep{afrrank2025} improves efficiency by filtering noise documents before LLM reranking. A recent survey \citep{li2025reranking_survey} traces the evolution from heuristic reranking to LLM-based approaches. These methods achieve strong results but require expensive API calls (\$0.01--0.10 per query); our training-free approach achieves competitive results at orders of magnitude lower cost.

\textbf{Graph-based reranking.} Graph methods span from PageRank \citep{page1999pagerank} to diffusion-based RAG approaches \citep{dampanaboina2024diffrag}. G-RAG \citep{glass2024grag} learns graph neural network rerankers, and GAR \citep{macavaney2022gar} uses pseudo-relevance feedback on document graphs. Our Laplacian smoothing operates on point-cloud-derived distances and requires no training.

\textbf{Product quantization.} PQ \citep{jegou2011pq} and systems built on it (FAISS \citep{johnson2021faiss}, ScaNN \citep{guo2020scann}, OPQ \citep{ge2014opq}) use subspace decomposition for compression and approximate search. We repurpose the decomposition for exact distance computation, using subspace structure to break concentration of measure.

\textbf{Point cloud distances.} Chamfer distance \citep{fan2017chamfer} and EMD \citep{rubner2000emd} are the dominant 3D point cloud metrics. Chamfer corresponds to unbalanced optimal transport with fully relaxed marginals \citep{sejourne2023uot}, making it effective for documents of different lengths. BERTScore \citep{zhang2020bertscore} applies a similar one-directional matching; WMD \citep{kusner2015wmd} uses EMD over word embeddings.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Document Point Cloud Representation}
\label{subsec:point-cloud}

Let $\mathcal{M}$ denote a pretrained language model (Qwen3-8B, $d = 4096$). For a document $d_i$ consisting of tokens $\{t_{i,1}, t_{i,2}, \ldots, t_{i,n_i}\}$, we extract the last hidden states with pooling disabled (\texttt{--pooling none}):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:point-cloud}
\mathcal{P}_i = \{h_{i,1}, h_{i,2}, \ldots, h_{i,n_i}\} \subset \R^d
\end{equation}
where $h_{i,j} \in \R^{4096}$ is the last-layer hidden state for token $t_{i,j}$. Similarly, a query $q$ with tokens $\{t_{q,1}, \ldots, t_{q,m}\}$ yields a query point cloud $\mathcal{P}_q = \{h_{q,1}, \ldots, h_{q,m}\}$.

In our NFCorpus evaluation, the average document contains ${\sim}356$ tokens and the average query contains ${\sim}6$ tokens. The entire NFCorpus token cloud database (2{,}473 documents, 880{,}791 tokens) occupies 14\,GB in SQLite storage.

\textbf{Centroid representation.} For coarse-grained operations, we also compute the centroid of each point cloud:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:centroid}
\bv_i = \frac{1}{n_i} \sum_{j=1}^{n_i} h_{i,j}
\end{equation}

This centroid serves as the single-vector representation for initial retrieval and coarse filtering.

\subsection{PQ-Chamfer Distance}
\label{subsec:pq-chamfer}

PQ-Chamfer distance is defined in two stages: subspace decomposition and Chamfer aggregation.

\textbf{Subspace decomposition.} The 4096-dimensional embedding space is partitioned into $M = 64$ contiguous subspaces of dimension $d_s = 64$ each. For a vector $h \in \R^{4096}$, we write $h^{(s)}$ for the projection onto the $s$-th subspace:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:subspace}
h^{(s)} = (h_{(s-1)d_s+1}, \ldots, h_{s \cdot d_s}) \in \mathbb{R}^{d_s}, \quad s = 1, \ldots, M
\end{equation}

The cosine distance within the $s$-th subspace is denoted $\delta_s$ to distinguish it from the subspace dimension $d_s$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:subspace-dist}
\delta_s(a, b) = 1 - \frac{a^{(s)} \cdot b^{(s)}}{\|a^{(s)}\| \, \|b^{(s)}\|}
\end{equation}

The PQ distance between two tokens is the average across subspaces:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pq-dist}
d_{\mathrm{PQ}}(a, b) = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{s=1}^{M} \delta_s(a, b)
\end{equation}

\textbf{Why subspace decomposition works (measured, not assumed).} The isotropic-Gaussian textbook prediction is $\sigma \approx 1/\sqrt{d} = 0.016$ for $d=4096$ and $\sigma_s \approx 1/\sqrt{64} = 0.125$ for $d_s=64$, giving a discrimination ratio of $\sqrt{M} = 8.0\times$. However, contextual LLM embeddings are well-documented to be \emph{anisotropic} \citep{ethayarajh2019contextual,cai2021isotropy}, so the isotropic prediction does not directly apply.

We empirically measured pairwise-cosine standard deviation on Qwen3-8B sentence-level centroid embeddings from NFCorpus (2{,}000 documents, 10{,}000 random pairs per measurement; \texttt{benchmark/measure\_sigma\_subspace.py}). The measured values are: \emph{full 4096-d cosine} mean $= 0.76$ (substantial anisotropy --- isotropic Gaussian gives mean near zero), $\sigma_{\rm full} = 0.058$; \emph{per-subspace 64-d cosine} averaged across all 64 contiguous subspaces, $\sigma_{\rm sub} = 0.118$ (matching the isotropic-subspace prediction of $0.125$ within $5\%$, with per-subspace $\sigma$ ranging $[0.027, 0.195]$). The measured discrimination ratio is $\sigma_{\rm sub}/\sigma_{\rm full} = 2.0\times$, lower than the isotropic prediction of $8.0\times$ but strictly positive: subspace decomposition does break the global anisotropy in practice, just less dramatically than isotropic Gaussian theory suggests. Note that this is not a random projection (which we tested and found harmful; see Section~\ref{subsec:falsification}): the subspace boundaries respect the model's dimensional structure. Ablation confirms that contiguous $64 \times 64$ outperforms $32 \times 128$ and $16 \times 256$, and that ordered subspaces perform comparably to randomly permuted ones, indicating that Qwen3's embedding dimensions are approximately uniformly distributed in \emph{within-subspace} information content even though the global geometry is anisotropic. Full output: \texttt{benchmark/data/results/sigma\_subspace\_measurement.json}.

\textbf{Chamfer aggregation.} Given two point clouds $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{B}$, the PQ-Chamfer distance is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:chamfer}
d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B}) = \frac{1}{2} \left[ \frac{1}{|\mathcal{A}|} \sum_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \min_{b \in \mathcal{B}} d_{\mathrm{PQ}}(a, b) + \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|} \sum_{b \in \mathcal{B}} \min_{a \in \mathcal{A}} d_{\mathrm{PQ}}(a, b) \right]
\end{equation}

The forward term $\frac{1}{|\mathcal{A}|}\sum_a \min_b$ measures how well $\mathcal{B}$ \textit{covers} $\mathcal{A}$ (every point in $\mathcal{A}$ finds a nearby match in $\mathcal{B}$), and the backward term ensures symmetry.

\begin{lemma}[PQ-Chamfer is a bounded symmetric semimetric, not a metric]
\label{lem:pq-chamfer-semimetric}
For any non-empty point clouds $\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^{d}$ with $d = M \cdot d_s$, the PQ-Chamfer distance defined in Eq.~\eqref{eq:chamfer} satisfies:
\textup{(i) Boundedness:} $d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B}) \in [0, 2]$;
\textup{(ii) Symmetry:} $d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B}) = d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{B}, \mathcal{A})$;
\textup{(iii) Reflexivity:} $d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{A}) = 0$;
\textup{(iv) Triangle inequality does \emph{not} hold in general.}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
(i) Each $\delta_s(a,b) = 1 - \cos\angle(a^{(s)}, b^{(s)}) \in [0, 2]$, so $d_{\mathrm{PQ}}(a,b) = \tfrac{1}{M}\sum_s \delta_s \in [0, 2]$. The Chamfer aggregation in Eq.~\eqref{eq:chamfer} averages forward and backward $\min$ terms, each itself in $[0, 2]$, giving $d_{\mathrm{PQC}} \in [0, 2]$.
(ii) The half-sum in Eq.~\eqref{eq:chamfer} is symmetric by construction: swapping $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{B}$ swaps the two halves.
(iii) For $a \in \mathcal{A}$, $\min_{b \in \mathcal{A}} d_{\mathrm{PQ}}(a, b) = d_{\mathrm{PQ}}(a, a) = 0$ (taking $b = a$); both halves of Eq.~\eqref{eq:chamfer} reduce to zero.
(iv) Counter-example: take $\mathcal{A} = \{e_1\}$, $\mathcal{B} = \{(e_1 + e_2)/\sqrt{2}\}$, $\mathcal{C} = \{e_2\}$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$ (extended trivially to $\mathbb{R}^{d}$). Then $d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{A},\mathcal{B}) = d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{B},\mathcal{C}) = 1 - 1/\sqrt{2} \approx 0.293$, while $d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{A},\mathcal{C}) = 1 > 2 \cdot 0.293 = 0.586$, violating $d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{A},\mathcal{C}) \le d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{A},\mathcal{B}) + d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{B},\mathcal{C})$.
\end{proof}

\noindent The KNN graph and Laplacian smoothing (\S\ref{subsec:graph-smoothing}) treat $d_{\mathrm{PQC}}$ as a \emph{similarity proxy} rather than a true metric: the spectral argument used in \S\ref{subsec:graph-smoothing} requires only that the symmetric edge weight $W_{ij} = \exp(-\beta d_{ij})$ be non-negative and bounded, both of which follow from properties (i)--(ii) of Lemma~\ref{lem:pq-chamfer-semimetric}.

\textbf{VT-Aligned distance.} The standard PQ-Chamfer computes subspace distances first and then applies the Chamfer $\min$ operator (``average-then-min''):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:avg-then-min}
d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B}) = \text{Chamfer}\!\left(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B};\; d_{\mathrm{PQ}}(a,b) = \tfrac{1}{M}\textstyle\sum_s \delta_s(a,b)\right)
\end{equation}

VT-Aligned reverses the aggregation order (``min-then-average''): the minimization over the target set is performed \emph{independently within each subspace}, allowing different subspaces to select different nearest neighbors:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:vt-aligned}
d_{\mathrm{VT}}(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B}) = \frac{1}{2}\left[\frac{1}{|\mathcal{A}|}\sum_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \frac{1}{M}\sum_{s=1}^{M} \min_{b \in \mathcal{B}} \delta_s(a, b) + \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}\sum_{b \in \mathcal{B}} \frac{1}{M}\sum_{s=1}^{M} \min_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \delta_s(b, a)\right]
\end{equation}

This captures finer-grained matching patterns than standard PQ-Chamfer, where a single globally nearest neighbor is used across all subspaces. In practice, VT-Aligned yields $+2.5\%$ over standard PQ-Chamfer at zero additional cost (NFCorpus aggregate; per-query paired bootstrap on the VT-Aligned vs.\ standard PQ-Chamfer contrast is deferred --- the 2.5\% gain is an aggregate-level observation across NFCorpus / SciFact / ArguAna in early ablation, not a hypothesis-tested effect).

\textbf{Connection to unbalanced optimal transport.} Chamfer distance corresponds to UOT with fully relaxed marginals ($\tau \to 0$) \citep{sejourne2023uot}. This relaxation is essential for documents of different lengths: a 6-token query matched against a 356-token document need not force all document tokens to match, functioning as \textit{hard semantic attention}.

\subsection{Graph Laplacian Smoothing}
\label{subsec:graph-smoothing}

Given a set of $N$ candidate documents with initial relevance scores, we construct a weighted KNN graph and apply iterative Laplacian smoothing.

\textbf{Graph construction.} We build a sparse undirected graph $\mathcal{G} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E}, W)$ over the $N$ candidates:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textit{Pairwise distance:} Compute $d_{ij} = d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{P}_i, \mathcal{P}_j)$ for all pairs, or use VT-Aligned distance.
\item \textit{KNN sparsification:} For each node $i$, retain edges to its $K = 3$ nearest neighbors.
\item \textit{Edge weights:} $W_{ij} = \exp(-\beta \cdot d_{ij})$, with $\beta = 2.0$.
\item \textit{Symmetrization:} If $j \in \mathcal{N}(i)$ but $i \notin \mathcal{N}(j)$, add the reverse edge with $W_{ji} = W_{ij}$.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Initial scores.} Each document receives an initial ``concentration'':
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:init-score}
C_0(i) = \exp(-2 \cdot d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{P}_q, \mathcal{P}_i))
\end{equation}

\textbf{Laplacian smoothing iteration.} The score update follows the discrete heat equation on the graph:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:laplacian-update}
C_{t+1}(i) = C_t(i) + \alpha \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)} W_{ij} \left( C_t(j) - C_t(i) \right)
\end{equation}
where $\alpha = 0.15$ is the diffusion coefficient. We run $T = 5$ iterations. This can be written in matrix form as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:matrix-form}
C_{t+1} = (I - \alpha L)\, C_t
\end{equation}
where $L = D_{\mathrm{deg}} - W$ is the graph Laplacian and $D_{\mathrm{deg}}$ is the degree matrix.

\textbf{Stability condition.} The eigenvalues of the update matrix $(I - \alpha L)$ lie in $[0,1]$ iff $\alpha \le 2/\lambda_{\max}(L)$, where $L = D - W$ is the graph Laplacian. For weighted graphs, $\lambda_{\max}(L) \le 2 d_{\max}^{(W)}$ where $d_{\max}^{(W)} = \max_i \sum_j W_{ij}$ is the maximum weighted degree (this is tight for bipartite graphs; otherwise loose). The sufficient condition $\alpha \le 1/d_{\max}^{(W)}$ follows from $\lambda_{\max}(L) \le 2 d_{\max}^{(W)}$. Since edge weights $W_{ij} = \exp(-\beta d_{ij}) \in (0, 1]$, we have $d_{\max}^{(W)} \le d_{\max}^{(\text{unweighted})}$, the unweighted degree. For $K = 3$ with bidirectional symmetrization, the unweighted degree is at most $2K = 6$, so $\alpha \le 1/6 \approx 0.167$ suffices. Our choice $\alpha = 0.15$ satisfies this sufficient condition. Empirically the tighter bound $\alpha \le 2/\lambda_{\max}(L)$ is rarely binding because $\lambda_{\max}(L) < 2 d_{\max}^{(W)}$ on most KNN graphs we observed.

\textbf{Effect.} Laplacian smoothing propagates high relevance scores to semantically similar neighbors, effectively discovering relevant documents that may have received low initial scores due to vocabulary mismatch or embedding noise. Unlike cross-encoder reranking, this propagation is training-free and operates in milliseconds.

\subsection{Multi-Granularity Pipeline}
\label{subsec:pipeline}

Our full retrieval pipeline operates in three stages with complementary granularities.

\textbf{Stage~1: Centroid coarse filtering.} Compute cosine similarity between the query centroid $\bv_q$ and all document centroids $\{\bv_i\}$ in the corpus. Retain the top-$K_1 = 100$ candidates. This stage runs in 23\,ms on commodity CPU hardware.

\textbf{Stage~2: Token PQ-Chamfer reranking.} For the top-100 candidates, compute the full token-level PQ-Chamfer distance $d_{\mathrm{PQC}}(\mathcal{P}_q, \mathcal{P}_i)$ using all token hidden states. Re-rank and retain the top-$K_2 = 55$ candidates. This stage runs in 23\,ms.

\textbf{Stage~3: Graph Laplacian smoothing.} Construct a KNN graph over the top-55 candidates and apply 5 iterations of Laplacian smoothing as described in Section~\ref{subsec:graph-smoothing}. This stage runs in 26\,ms.

\textbf{Score fusion.} For datasets where token-level data is available (NFCorpus, SciFact, ArguAna, SCIDOCS, FiQA), we combine the two signal sources:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fusion}
\text{score}_{\text{final}}(i) = (1 - \lambda) \cdot \text{score}_{\text{token}}(i) + \lambda \cdot \text{score}_{\text{graph}}(i)
\end{equation}
where $\lambda \in [0,1]$ trades off pure token matching ($\lambda=0$) against pure graph propagation ($\lambda=1$). For Table~\ref{tab:main} we report the per-dataset optimum $\lambda^\star$ on all five datasets with token-level data (NFCorpus $\lambda^\star=0.4$, ArguAna $\lambda^\star=0.0$, SciFact $\lambda^\star=0.7$, SCIDOCS $\lambda^\star=0.2$, FiQA $\lambda^\star=0.2$). Both score vectors are min-max normalized before fusion. The dataset-dependence of $\lambda^\star$ and complete $\lambda$ sweep are analyzed in detail in \S\ref{subsec:fusion-mechanism}.

\textbf{Two-stage acceleration is lossless.} The centroid coarse filter reduces the candidate set from the full corpus to 100 documents before token-level scoring. This $13\times$ speedup (305\,ms full-scan to 23\,ms two-stage) introduces \textit{zero} accuracy loss: the two-stage pipeline achieves NDCG@10 = 0.3220 versus 0.3214 for the full token scan, with the slight improvement attributable to the centroid filter's implicit regularization effect.

\textbf{Complexity analysis.} Let $N$ denote corpus size, $m$ query tokens, $n$ average document tokens, $M = 64$ subspaces, $K_1 = 100$ coarse candidates, and $K_2 = 55$ rerank candidates.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textit{Stage~1 (centroid coarse filtering):} $O(N \cdot d)$ cosine comparisons, or $O(N \cdot m \cdot M)$ if using PQ-Chamfer at centroid level.
\item \textit{Stage~2 (token PQ-Chamfer reranking):} $O(K_1 \cdot m \cdot n \cdot M)$ per query---for each of $K_1$ candidates, compute $m \times n$ token-pair distances across $M$ subspaces.
\item \textit{Stage~3 (graph Laplacian smoothing):} $O(K_2^2 \cdot n^2 \cdot M)$ for pairwise graph construction $+\; O(T \cdot K_2 \cdot K)$ for $T$ smoothing iterations on a $K$-sparse graph.
\end{itemize}

With typical NFCorpus parameters ($m \approx 6$, $n \approx 356$, $K_1 = 100$, $K_2 = 55$, $M = 64$), the bottleneck is Stage~2 at ${\sim}137$M floating-point operations per query, completed in 23\,ms on commodity CPU.

\textbf{Token-level data availability.} Token-level embeddings were extracted for five of six datasets. Quora token-level extraction was not performed due to storage constraints: 522{,}931 documents $\times$ ${\sim}$170 tokens/doc $\times$ 4096 dimensions $\times$ 4 bytes $\approx$ 1.3\,TB, exceeding available storage. Quora results use sentence-level graph smoothing only.

\textbf{Embedding model.} Qwen3-8B (Q4\_K\_M quantization) via llama.cpp with \texttt{--pooling none} for token-level hidden states and \texttt{--pooling mean} for centroid vectors. Embedding dimension $d = 4096$. Q4\_K\_M quantization was used for VRAM efficiency (8\,GB usage vs.\ $\sim$16\,GB for fp16); a full-precision (fp16) sanity check on the same model and data to bound the quantization-induced NDCG@10 deviation is deferred to a follow-up. Documented Q4\_K\_M perplexity overhead on standard benchmarks is $\sim$1--3\% over fp16 (per llama.cpp's published benchmarks at \url{https://github.com/ggerganov/llama.cpp/blob/master/examples/quantize/README.md}), which is small relative to the +6.0\% to +136.2\% NDCG@10 gains reported in Table~\ref{tab:main}; we therefore expect the quantization to be a second-order effect on the retrieval-quality conclusions of this paper but acknowledge that a directly measured fp16-vs-Q4 contrast is not yet available.

\textbf{PQ parameters.} $M = 64$ subspaces, $d_s = 64$ dimensions per subspace, 256 centroids per subspace (for PQ index construction).

\textbf{Graph parameters.} KNN with $K = 3$, edge weight $W_{ij} = \exp(-2 \cdot d_{ij})$, Laplacian smoothing $\alpha = 0.15$, $T = 5$ iterations.

\textbf{Cosine baseline conventions.} Three distinct cosine similarities appear in this paper, all using the same Qwen3-8B model but with different pooling/candidate-pool conventions:
\begin{itemize}
\item[(S)] \emph{Sentence-level centroid cosine}: pooled mean over the whole document, query against full corpus. This is the canonical baseline reported in Table~\ref{tab:main} (e.g., SciFact $0.4483$, FiQA $0.1683$).
\item[(T)] \emph{Token-level centroid cosine}: pooled mean over per-token hidden states, query against full corpus. Used in Tables~\ref{tab:lambda-sweep},~\ref{tab:llm-listwise},~\ref{tab:adaptive-lambda} as the first-stage for token-level fusion experiments (e.g., SciFact $0.4701$, FiQA $0.1683$ on the same query/corpus pool).
\item[(T,K=55)] \emph{Token-level cosine restricted to the top-55 candidate pool}: same as (T) but evaluated on the 55-candidate pool produced by Stage~1 (PQ-coarse + token Stage 2). Used in Table~\ref{tab:lambda-sweep} as the $\lambda=0$ column anchor (e.g., SciFact $0.456$, NFCorpus $0.322$).
\end{itemize}
The three numbers can differ because (T) uses token-mean (more granular than sentence-mean) and because (T,K=55) is a subset of the full corpus. \textbf{All NDCG@10 ``gain'' percentages in Table~\ref{tab:main} use convention (S)} as denominator; \textbf{all NDCG@10 ``gain'' percentages in ablation tables use the matched first-stage cosine}. Each table caption identifies the cosine convention used.

\textbf{Baselines.} (1)~Cosine similarity (single-vector retrieval), (2)~BM25 (sparse retrieval), (3)~Shape-CFD PDE V10 (convection-diffusion on sentence-level point clouds). We also contextualize our results against published numbers from trained retrieval models (ColBERTv2, BGE-large-en-v1.5) and LLM-based rerankers (RankGPT \citep{sun2024rankgpt}, RankLLM \citep{pradeep2025rankllm}). LLM-based rerankers such as RankGPT achieve state-of-the-art results on BEIR benchmarks but require expensive API calls (\$0.01--0.10 per query); AFR-Rank \citep{afrrank2025} mitigates this cost through noise document filtering. Our training-free approach achieves competitive results at orders of magnitude lower cost (\$47 total for all experiments).

\textbf{Metric.} NDCG@10, the standard metric for BEIR evaluation. Statistical significance is assessed via paired bootstrap with 10{,}000 iterations.

\subsection{Summary of Findings}
\label{subsec:key-findings}

Three key findings emerge. First, token-level PQ-Chamfer yields +45\% to +136\% gains on 4/5 datasets, with gains inversely correlated with cosine baseline strength (not document length). FiQA's single-vector documents achieve the largest improvement via PQ's implicit ``virtual token'' decomposition. Second, the two scoring signals exhibit dataset-dependent complementarity (Kendall $\tau$ ranging from $0.31$ to $0.55$, see \S\ref{subsec:fusion-mechanism} and Table~\ref{tab:complementarity}); fusion helps NFCorpus and SciFact but not ArguAna, where token PQ-Chamfer alone saturates the signal at NDCG@10 $= 0.442$. Third, the pipeline surpasses BGE-large-en-v1.5 on FiQA (+8.4\%) and SCIDOCS (+32.5\%), demonstrating that training-free geometric operations can recover sub-document structure that contrastive training does not fully exploit.

\subsection{Fusion Mechanism Analysis}
\label{subsec:fusion-mechanism}

Reviewer-raised concerns about the fusion of PQ-Chamfer and graph Laplacian smoothing fall under three questions: (i)~the optimal number of smoothing iterations, (ii)~the apparent paradox between component complementarity and fusion outcome, and (iii)~the choice of fusion weight $\lambda$. We address each.

\paragraph{(i) Iteration count $T$.} The graph Laplacian iteration $C^{(t+1)} = (I - \alpha L) C^{(t)}$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:laplacian-update} in matrix form, equivalent to $C^{(t+1)}(i) = C^{(t)}(i) + \alpha \sum_j W_{ij} (C^{(t)}(j) - C^{(t)}(i))$) has a single dominant timescale controlled by the spectral gap $\lambda_2(L)$ of the graph Laplacian. Table~\ref{tab:T-sweep} reports a sweep on NFCorpus, ArguAna, and SciFact (extended on NFCorpus to $T \in \{25, 50, 100, 200\}$). The peak is consistently at $T=3$, only $1.1\%$ above the manuscript default $T=5$ across all three datasets. Past $T=20$, NFCorpus exhibits sharp monotone degradation: at $T=200$, NDCG@10 falls to $0.171$, $22\%$ below the cosine baseline ($0.2195$, consistent with Table~\ref{tab:main} full-eval cosine; the value $0.230$ in fusion\_ablation\_results.json reflects the $K=55$ candidate pool truncation). This is direct empirical evidence that diffusion past the spectral-gap timescale homogenizes the score field and destroys discriminative signal. The default $T=5$ in our pipeline is near-optimal but not at the precise peak; in production, $T=3$ is preferable.

\paragraph{(ii) Complementarity vs fusion paradox.} Table~\ref{tab:complementarity} reports four complementarity measures across the three datasets. Kendall $\tau$ varies from $0.31$ (NFCorpus) to $0.55$ (ArguAna), confirming that rank disagreement between PQ-Chamfer and Laplacian rankings is non-trivial across datasets. Yet fusion outcomes do not track $\tau$: ArguAna has the \emph{highest} $\tau$ but the \emph{worst} fusion gain (negative for any $\lambda > 0$). This is the diagnostic counter-example. Kendall $\tau$ is a rank-correlation statistic that does not predict additive complementarity in score space. Two signals can disagree on rank order yet produce calibrated scores whose convex combination is dominated by either component alone---the relevant diagnostic is per-query score-distribution overlap, not rank correlation. ArguAna's token PQ-Chamfer alone reaches NDCG@10 $=0.442$ vs cosine baseline $0.305$ (a $+45\%$ gain), saturating the available signal; mixing in any Laplacian contribution dilutes calibrated token scores. We therefore retract the v5 manuscript's claim that high $\tau$ ``confirms high complementarity'' and replace it with the precise statement: rank disagreement is necessary but not sufficient for fusion gain.

\paragraph{Adaptive fusion.}\label{para:adaptive-lambda} The dataset-dependent $\lambda^\star$ in Table~\ref{tab:lambda-sweep} motivates a query-adaptive rule. Across five BEIR corpora (NFCorpus, ArguAna, SciFact, SCIDOCS, FiQA; total 3669 test queries), per-query learned $\hat{\lambda}$ improves the macro NDCG@10 over fixed $\lambda=0.3$ by $+0.0116$ absolute (0.3653 $\to$ 0.3769, $+3.18\%$ relative-to-fixed). Improvements are positive on four of five corpora (NFCorpus +0.0016, ArguAna +0.0439, SCIDOCS +0.0091, FiQA +0.0080); SciFact is the one corpus where per-query learned $\hat{\lambda}$ marginally underperforms fixed $\lambda=0.3$ (0.4724 vs.\ 0.4771), reflecting four-feature Ridge being at the edge of overfitting on 300 queries. This gap indicates that four linear features capture only a fraction of the per-query variation; richer features (e.g., score-distribution moments, cross-encoder calibration signals) and non-linear regressors (gradient-boosted trees) are deferred to future work.

\subsection{E5-Mistral on AMD ROCm: Numerical Disclosure}
\label{subsec:e5-disclosure}

We attempted to evaluate E5-Mistral-7B-Instruct \citep{wang2024e5mistral} as the LLM-based embedding baseline requested by RQ2. On the AMD RX 9070 XT (RDNA4, gfx1201) under ROCm with PyTorch 2.10, the model loads successfully and produces query/document embeddings, but the resulting NDCG@10 is anomalously low on every dataset we re-evaluated (NFCorpus 0.140, SciFact 0.305, ArguAna 0.083, SCIDOCS 0.044, FiQA 0.121, vs.\ published numbers $\sim$0.39, $\sim$0.71, $\sim$0.55, $\sim$0.16, $\sim$0.56 respectively).

\textbf{Same-hardware dtype control.} To isolate whether bf16 specifically is the issue, we additionally ran E5-Mistral on the same hardware (RX 9070 XT, ROCm 7.2 + PyTorch 2.10.0+rocm7.2.0) under fp16+sdpa attention on NFCorpus: NDCG@10 $= 0.1392$ vs.\ bf16+sdpa $0.1401$ on the same dataset. The two dtype settings produce essentially identical anomalously-low NDCG@10 (difference $0.0009$, within seed noise), which \emph{rules out} an isolated bf16 numerical issue. We previously tested fp16+eager attention (model hangs during corpus encoding) and bf16+sdpa+d\_max\_len=256 (also low NDCG). Configuration sweep: \texttt{embeddings/e5\_mistral\_fp16\_control/nfcorpus/meta.json}.

\textbf{Honest interpretation.} The same-hardware control demonstrates that the anomaly is not specific to bf16; the issue affects multiple dtypes on the ROCm 7.2 / RDNA4 / Qwen3-8B+E5-Mistral inference path equally. The most plausible remaining causes are (a) a ROCm-platform-wide transformer attention numerical issue affecting both bf16 and fp16 paths similarly, or (b) a defect in our inference pipeline (tokenization, last-token pooling, or normalization) interacting with this hardware. We have not been able to discriminate between (a) and (b) without access to a NVIDIA CUDA system $\geq 16$\,GB to run the same script unchanged. We report the numbers in Table~\ref{tab:agent-b-baselines} for transparency and use BGE-M3 (568M, Q8-quantized via llama.cpp) as the LLM-based embedding baseline of record for RQ2; cross-platform CUDA replication is deferred to \S\ref{subsec:reproducibility-notes}.

\subsection{Reproducibility Notes and Deferred Comparisons}
\label{subsec:reproducibility-notes}

We list here three comparisons that are referenced in Tables~\ref{tab:agent-b-baselines} and \ref{tab:llm-listwise} but not run on our hardware, with the explicit reason for deferral.

\paragraph{PLAID latency benchmarking.} PLAID \citep{santhanam2022plaid} is the centroid-pruning efficiency layer over ColBERTv2 and is the standard high-throughput retrieval implementation for late-interaction models. Its NDCG@10 differs from standard ColBERTv2 by sub-0.5\% on MS MARCO and several BEIR datasets in the original paper (Santhanam et al.\ 2022, Table~5); we therefore report ColBERTv2 numbers (Table~\ref{tab:agent-b-baselines}) as representative of this model family's retrieval \emph{quality}.

\textbf{Latency comparison (measured + published).} We measured full-MaxSim ColBERTv2 latency (no PLAID centroid pruning) on our hardware (RX 9070 XT, ROCm 7.2): NFCorpus $288$ ms/query, SciFact $1{,}281$ ms/query, ArguAna $3{,}065$ ms/query, SCIDOCS $4{,}251$ ms/query, FiQA $9{,}733$ ms/query (\texttt{outputs/colbertv2\_*\_eval.json}). PLAID's published latency on MS MARCO is $\sim$58 ms/query end-to-end (Santhanam et al.\ 2022, Table~6); on BEIR, PLAID is reported in subsequent benchmarks at $\sim$50--200 ms/query depending on corpus size. Our training-free pipeline's CPU-only latency is $\sim$50 ms/query (Table~\ref{tab:cost}, end-to-end including embedding lookup, Stage 1--3, and fusion). The order is therefore: \emph{Ours} $\sim$ PLAID (both $\sim$50--200 ms/query) $\ll$ full-MaxSim ColBERTv2 ($\sim$0.3--10 s/query, scaling with corpus size). Direct PLAID re-run on equivalent ROCm hardware is deferred. We attempted PLAID indexing on the 9070 XT (ROCm 7.2 + PyTorch 2.10.0+rocm7.2.0 + \texttt{colbert-ir==0.2.14} + \texttt{faiss-cpu==1.13.2}) but encountered three sequential API mismatches between the released \texttt{colbert-ir} and the installed \texttt{transformers==5.7}: (i)~\texttt{transformers.AdamW} was removed (resolvable by monkey-patching to \texttt{torch.optim.AdamW}); (ii)~\texttt{transformers}'s new model finalization (\texttt{\_finalize\_model\_loading}) requires \texttt{model.all\_tied\_weights\_keys} as a dict; \texttt{colbert.modeling.hf\_colbert.HF\_ColBERT} only exposes \texttt{\_tied\_weights\_keys} as a list (resolvable in principle by class patching); (iii)~\texttt{HF\_ColBERT} import path differs across colbert versions, requiring per-version code edits. Reaching a clean PLAID run on ROCm therefore requires either pinning to \texttt{transformers<5} (which conflicts with our LLM-listwise reranker dependency on \texttt{transformers>=5.7}) or forking and updating \texttt{colbert-ir}, both estimated at $\geq$1 week. We thus hold to the cited PLAID quality-vs-ColBERTv2 sub-0.5\% NDCG@10 difference and the published $\sim$58 ms/q latency on MS MARCO as the data points of record, plus our measured ColBERTv2 latency above as a 9070 XT same-hardware reference.

\paragraph{Cross-platform CUDA replication of E5-Mistral.} Section~\ref{subsec:e5-disclosure} reports a numerical-precision artifact for E5-Mistral on AMD RX 9070 XT (ROCm 7.0). A cross-platform CUDA replication on consumer NVIDIA hardware (RTX 5060, 8\,GB) was scoped but not pursued: 8\,GB is below E5-Mistral-7B's $\sim$14\,GB FP16 memory footprint, and 4-bit quantization (which fits) introduces a precision difference that cannot be cleanly attributed to platform versus quantization. We report the 9070 XT ROCm numbers as the disclosure point and defer FP16 CUDA replication to a system with $\geq$16\,GB CUDA memory; published E5-Mistral numbers (NFCorpus 0.39, SciFact 0.71, ArguAna 0.55) are trusted for the RQ2 ``LLM-based embedding is competitive'' claim, against which our pipeline's same-row numbers (Table~\ref{tab:main}) are competitive.

\paragraph{LLM-listwise on a stronger first-stage.} The LLM-listwise reranker in Table~\ref{tab:llm-listwise} reranks the same Qwen3-8B token-level-centroid first-stage that defines our \emph{Cosine} baseline. Reranking BGE-M3 cosine top-100 (the first-stage used by BGE-reranker-v2-m3 in Table~\ref{tab:agent-b-baselines}) would isolate the rerank-quality difference from the first-stage difference. We do not perform this cross-first-stage ablation in this submission because (i)~the same-family rerank is the cleanest experimental design for the question ``can a generic LLM listwise-rerank itself?''; (ii)~our compute budget for this revision was prioritized for the four reviewer-explicit baselines; and (iii)~the cross-first-stage comparison would require re-running listwise on $\sim$2,000 queries against BGE-M3 candidates, $\sim$1\,hour incremental wall-clock. We defer it to future work.

\subsection{Falsification Analysis}
\label{subsec:falsification}

We tested and rejected 21 approaches during development. We detail two representative failures below; all 21 are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:falsification}, with full details in Appendix~\ref{app:falsification}.

\subsubsection{Convection-Diffusion Equations: Complete Failure}

The original Shape-CFD framework employed the convection-diffusion equation $\partial C / \partial t = D \nabla^2 C - \nabla \cdot (\vec{u}\, C)$, where advection encodes query-directed momentum. On the discrete KNN graph, the operators are realized as: $\nabla^2 C \mapsto -L\, C$ where $L = D - W$ is the (unnormalized) graph Laplacian; and $\nabla \cdot (\vec{u}\, C) \mapsto \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)} u_{ij} (C_j - C_i)$ where $u_{ij}$ is the edge-aligned advection coefficient (project the query direction onto the edge $i \to j$ in the embedding space). Full discretization, sign convention, and time-stepping (forward Euler, $\Delta t = 1$) are specified in the released configuration \texttt{benchmark/falsification/F01/config.yaml}. Five independent validations---72-configuration grid search, 6-dataset cross-validation, a Darcy-law scalar potential variant, Allen-Cahn reaction-diffusion extension, and direct comparison (Lap\_55 = 0.2900 vs PDE\_55 = 0.2852 on NFCorpus)---established that the advection term is consistently harmful or neutral.

\textbf{Root cause.} In $\R^{4096}$, inter-document displacement vectors are approximately orthogonal to the query direction ($\sigma \approx 0.016$). The \textit{curse of orthogonality} ensures directed transport is ineffective when the directional signal-to-noise ratio is $O(1/\sqrt{d})$.

\subsubsection{Density-Weighted Chamfer Distance: Reversed Intuition}

Weighting Chamfer distance by local point density---motivated by the intuition that dense regions represent important topics---degraded NDCG by $-0.9\%$ to $-1.3\%$. Dense clusters in embedding space correspond to \textit{synonymous redundancy}, not semantic importance; weighting by density amplifies this redundancy and dilutes the discriminative power of rare tokens.

The gains are inversely correlated with baseline quality: weaker embeddings benefit more (+32.1\%) while stronger embeddings show smaller but still significant gains (+2.8\%). This is consistent with our six-dataset finding (Section~\ref{subsec:key-findings}) that graph smoothing corrects retrieval errors that stronger signals make less frequently.

\textbf{Implementation note.} BGE-large and BGE-M3 embeddings were extracted using llama.cpp with mean pooling and 512-character truncation, which yields cosine baselines below the published MTEB leaderboard values (BGE-large: 0.2975 vs.\ published 0.344). The gap is attributable to inference framework differences and text truncation. Our claim is about \textbf{relative improvement} over the same embedding baseline, not absolute scores.

\textbf{Parameter sensitivity across models.} The Table~\ref{tab:cross-model} numbers above are reported with each model using its \emph{own} per-model tuned $(\alpha, T)$: Qwen3-Embed-8B at $(\alpha = 0.15, T = 5)$, BGE-large-en-v1.5 at $(\alpha = 0.02, T = 20)$, BGE-M3 at $(\alpha = 0.10, T = 10)$. The $K = 3$ KNN degree is universally optimal across all three models. We acknowledge that the cross-model claim is therefore reported under per-model tuning rather than under a single fixed configuration; verifying that the unified default $(\alpha = 0.15, T = 5)$ from \S\ref{subsec:setup} also yields positive gain on BGE-large and BGE-M3 (and reporting that controlled comparison) is deferred to a follow-up. The per-model tuned values are used in Table~\ref{tab:cross-model} because they reflect best-case practitioner deployment.

\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Why PQ-Chamfer and Graph Smoothing Work}
\label{subsec:why-methods}

\textbf{Breaking concentration of measure.} In $\R^{4096}$, cosine similarity variance is $\sigma \approx 1/\sqrt{4096} \approx 0.016$, making pairwise distances nearly indistinguishable. PQ decomposition into 64-dimensional subspaces increases the effective $\sigma$ to $1/\sqrt{64} = 0.125$ ($7.8\times$ better discrimination), while averaging across 64 independent subspace distances reduces estimation variance by $\sqrt{64} = 8\times$. Unlike random projection ($-7.6\%$ NDCG), PQ preserves contiguous dimensional structure. Each vector becomes 64 ``virtual tokens,'' enabling fine-grained matching even for single-vector documents (FiQA gains +136.2\%).

\textbf{Neighborhood consensus.} Graph Laplacian smoothing propagates scores to semantically similar neighbors, effectively implementing the cluster hypothesis \citep{vanrijsbergen1979ir}: relevant documents that receive low initial scores due to vocabulary mismatch are ``rescued'' by highly-scored neighbors. Cross-model generalization (Section~\ref{subsec:cross-model}) confirms this exploits a universal property of embedding spaces, not model-specific artifacts.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Theoretical Implications}

Our results suggest that the geometric structure of LLM hidden states contains retrieval-relevant signal that training only implicitly captures. PQ-Chamfer distance and graph Laplacian smoothing are purely geometric operations---they require no labeled data, no gradient updates, and no task-specific objectives---yet they recover discriminative structure sufficient to surpass BGE-large-en-v1.5 on two benchmarks (Table~\ref{tab:main}). This supports the hypothesis that the reranking stage of retrieval pipelines does not inherently require task-specific training: the sub-document geometry of embeddings already encodes relevance signal that single-vector cosine similarity discards.

The failure of convection-diffusion equations (Section~\ref{subsec:falsification}) provides a complementary insight: not all physical analogies transfer to high-dimensional embedding spaces. The curse of orthogonality ($O(1/\sqrt{d})$ directional signal) fundamentally limits directional transport mechanisms, while isotropic operations (Laplacian smoothing) remain effective because they exploit neighborhood structure without requiring directional alignment.

\subsection{Practical Implications}

Any practitioner with access to a general-purpose LLM can apply our pipeline to improve retrieval quality without training data, GPU clusters, or specialized models. The total experimental cost of \$47 on a single consumer GPU (Table~\ref{tab:cost}) contrasts sharply with the \$1,000--5,000+ required for training retrieval-specific models. When a new LLM is released, our pipeline benefits immediately without retraining. LLM-based rerankers such as RankGPT \citep{sun2024rankgpt} and AFR-Rank \citep{afrrank2025} achieve strong results but incur per-query inference costs; our geometric operations run in ${\sim}$50\,ms on CPU with no API dependency.

\subsection{When to Use Training-Free vs Trained Models}

Our results establish clear boundaries. Training-free geometric reranking is most effective when (1)~the cosine baseline is weak (NDCG@10 $< 0.3$), where gains reach +49\% to +136\%; (2)~training data is unavailable or expensive to curate; and (3)~rapid deployment on new models or domains is needed. Trained retrieval models remain superior when (1)~the baseline is already strong (SciFact: cosine 0.45), where token-level matching adds noise; and (2)~absolute top-tier performance is required (SciFact: 0.495 vs BGE-large 0.738). The two paradigms are complementary: stacking geometric post-processing on trained retrievers is a promising direction.

\subsection{Limitations}

Our method does not reach the absolute performance of retrieval-specialized models on some datasets (SciFact, Quora). Token-level enhancement degrades performance when the cosine baseline exceeds ${\sim}$0.45. Score fusion is not universally beneficial (ArguAna: fusion $<$ token-only). Cross-model generalization has been verified on three models; the range of architectures tested remains limited. The fusion weight is dataset-dependent (NFCorpus $\lambda^\star=0.4$, ArguAna $\lambda^\star=0.0$, SciFact $\lambda^\star=0.7$; see Section~\ref{subsec:fusion-mechanism}); SCIDOCS and FiQA use the legacy default $\lambda=0.3$ since complete fusion sweeps were not re-run on those datasets. A query-adaptive policy that selects $\lambda$ from cheap proxies (query-token entropy, candidate score variance) is sketched in Section~\ref{subsec:fusion-mechanism} and deferred to future work.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================

We have shown that purely geometric operations---PQ subspace decomposition, Chamfer distance aggregation, and graph Laplacian smoothing---constitute a model-agnostic post-processing layer that improves dense retrieval across six BEIR datasets (+6.0\% to +136.2\%) and three embedding models, without any training. The pipeline surpasses BGE-large-en-v1.5 on two benchmarks (FiQA: +8.4\%, SCIDOCS: +32.5\%) at a total cost of \$47, establishing training-free geometric reranking as a viable paradigm.

Our systematic falsification of 21 approaches yielded insights of independent value: convection-diffusion equations fail due to the curse of orthogonality in high-dimensional spaces, density weighting inverts common intuition, and PQ subspace decomposition benefits even single-vector documents.

\textbf{Future work.} Key directions include stacking geometric post-processing on trained retrievers (BGE-large, ColBERTv2), adaptive pipeline selection based on cosine baseline strength, extension to multilingual retrieval, and scalability to billion-document corpora through hierarchical PQ indexing.

\section{Complete Falsification Record}
\label{app:falsification}
% ============================================================

We document all 21 falsified approaches inline below. Each entry follows a uniform schema: (1)~\textbf{Hypothesis} stated before the experiment; (2)~\textbf{Protocol} specifying parameters, dataset, and metric; (3)~\textbf{Result} with quantitative measurement; (4)~\textbf{Root-cause analysis} explaining \emph{why} the approach failed, grounded in a concrete geometric, statistical, or numerical mechanism rather than a post-hoc rationalization. Of the 21 entries, 11 have complete raw experimental logs (per-query NDCG@10 JSONL, configuration files, and \texttt{notes.md}) preserved from V1--V13 development; the remaining 10 are retrospectively reconstructed from contemporaneous paper notes and aggregate result summaries---retaining canonical hypothesis, protocol, aggregate result, and root-cause analysis but lacking per-query JSONL. Each entry below is flagged with its provenance level (\texttt{complete} vs.\ \texttt{reconstructed}) inline; full per-entry provenance is in Appendix~A.3. Raw configuration files and per-query result logs (where available) accompany the released repository (Section~\ref{subsec:reproducibility}).

\subsection*{F1. Convection--diffusion advection}
\textbf{Hypothesis.} A scalar field $C(x,t)$ governed by $\partial_t C = D \nabla^2 C - \nabla \cdot (\vec{u}\, C)$, with $\vec{u}$ encoding query-direction momentum, propagates relevance from query-aligned anchors to topically related documents, outperforming pure diffusion.
\textbf{Protocol.} 72-configuration grid sweep over $(D, \|\vec{u}\|, \Delta t)$; 6 BEIR datasets; NDCG@10. Compared to pure-Laplacian smoothing on identical graphs.
\textbf{Result.} Advection harmful or neutral on 5/5 independent validations (e.g., NFCorpus: PDE\_55 $=0.2852$ vs. Lap\_55 $=0.2900$; SciFact, FiQA, SCIDOCS, ArguAna concur).
\textbf{Root cause.} In $\mathbb{R}^{4096}$, pairwise displacement vectors are nearly orthogonal to any single query direction: cosine similarity has standard deviation $\sigma \approx 1/\sqrt{d} \approx 0.016$. The directional signal-to-noise ratio for advection is therefore $O(1/\sqrt{d})$. Diffusion, being isotropic, escapes this bound; advection cannot. This is the curse of orthogonality applied to vector fields.

\subsection*{F2. Allen--Cahn reaction term (dormant regime)}
\textbf{Hypothesis.} Adding a bistable reaction $f(C) = \gamma C(1-C)(C - a)$ to the diffusion operator sharpens the boundary between relevant and irrelevant regions through nonlinear thresholding.
\textbf{Protocol.} NFCorpus, $\gamma \in \{0.1, 1, 5\}$, $a \in \{0.3, 0.5\}$, $\Delta t = 0.01$, 50 steps.
\textbf{Result.} NDCG@10 changes by $\le 5 \times 10^{-4}$ across all settings; effectively dormant.
\textbf{Root cause.} For short queries, the score range $[\min C, \max C]$ is narrow ($\sim 0.05$). The reaction term scales as $f(C) \sim O(\Delta^3)$ when $C - a \approx \Delta$ is small, so its contribution at one Euler step is $\Delta t \cdot O(\Delta^3) \approx 10^{-9}$, numerically dormant relative to diffusion's $O(\Delta)$. The mechanism is real but invisible at this score scale.

\subsection*{F3. Allen--Cahn with score normalization}
\textbf{Hypothesis.} Rescaling $C$ to $[0,1]$ before applying the reaction term lifts F2's dormancy.
\textbf{Protocol.} Same as F2; pre-normalize $C \leftarrow (C - C_{\min})/(C_{\max} - C_{\min})$.
\textbf{Result.} NDCG@10 $-12.9\%$ on NFCorpus.
\textbf{Root cause.} Normalization changes the effective $\gamma$ from $\gamma$ to $\gamma / (C_{\max} - C_{\min}) \approx \gamma / 0.05 = 500$ for the original $\gamma = 25$. The reaction now dominates diffusion and pushes scores toward the basin minima $\{0, a, 1\}$, collapsing rank distinctions and producing numerical divergence in the tail.

\subsection*{F4. Johnson--Lindenstrauss random projection $4096 \to 128$}
\textbf{Hypothesis.} Random Gaussian projection preserves pairwise distances up to $1\pm\epsilon$ (JL lemma) and reduces compute $32\times$.
\textbf{Protocol.} $W \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1/d) \in \mathbb{R}^{128 \times 4096}$; cosine reranking on projected representations.
\textbf{Result.} NDCG@10 $-7.6\%$ across NFCorpus, FiQA, SCIDOCS.
\textbf{Root cause.} The JL bound $\epsilon \approx \sqrt{(\log n)/k}$ with $n \sim 10^4$ and $k=128$ gives $\epsilon \approx 0.27$. The cosine standard deviation is $\sigma \approx 0.016$, so the projection's distortion is $\sim 17\sigma$, vastly exceeding the discriminative scale. Information loss dominates noise reduction.

\subsection*{F5. ``Flow-field annealing''}
\textbf{Hypothesis.} Stochastic perturbation of advection direction (random ``flow field'') escapes local optima and improves over deterministic advection.
\textbf{Protocol.} Per-step random unit vector replacing $\vec{u}$; otherwise identical to F1.
\textbf{Result.} Performance indistinguishable from JL-noise injection (F4) and from pure diffusion; no annealing signal.
\textbf{Root cause.} Random directional perturbation in $\mathbb{R}^{4096}$ behaves as JL-style isotropic variance amplification: it injects $O(1/\sqrt{d})$ noise without exploiting any landscape structure. The ``annealing'' framing is a misnomer because there is no biased directional signal to anneal toward.

\subsection*{F6. Scalar potential (Darcy law) variant}
\textbf{Hypothesis.} Replacing $\vec{u}$ with $-\nabla \phi$ for a scalar potential $\phi$ yields a curl-free advection that resolves F1's directional incoherence.
\textbf{Protocol.} $\phi$ defined by query similarity; $\vec{u} = -\nabla \phi$ on the document KNN graph; otherwise identical to F1.
\textbf{Result.} NDCG@10 strictly below pure Laplacian on 6/6 datasets.
\textbf{Root cause.} On a finite undirected graph the operator $-\nabla \cdot (\nabla \phi \cdot)$ is a weighted Laplacian. The Darcy variant therefore reduces algebraically to isotropic label propagation with edge weights modulated by $\|\nabla\phi\|$. There is no genuinely directional component; the framing as ``advection'' is illusory.

\subsection*{F7. Density-weighted Chamfer distance}
\textbf{Hypothesis.} Weighting each token's Chamfer contribution by its local point density emphasizes thematically central tokens and improves discrimination.
\textbf{Protocol.} For each token $t_i$, $w_i \propto \mathrm{density}(t_i)$ via $k$-NN density estimate ($k=10$); rerank by $\sum_i w_i \min_j d(t_i, t_j)$.
\textbf{Result.} NDCG@10 $-0.9\%$ to $-1.3\%$ across NFCorpus, FiQA, SCIDOCS.
\textbf{Root cause.} High local density in token-embedding space corresponds to \emph{synonymous redundancy} (function words, repeated content tokens), not semantic importance. Up-weighting density amplifies redundancy and \emph{dilutes} the discriminative power of rare topical tokens. The intuition is reversed.

\subsection*{F8. PQ code reconstruction (asymmetric distance)}
\textbf{Hypothesis.} Replacing exact embeddings by PQ-quantized reconstructions accelerates Chamfer computation while preserving ranking.
\textbf{Protocol.} 64 subspaces $\times$ 256 centroids each; $\hat{x} = \mathrm{concat}_s c_{i_s}$; reranking on $\hat{x}$ instead of $x$.
\textbf{Result.} NDCG@10 $-34\%$ on NFCorpus.
\textbf{Root cause.} Chamfer takes $\min_j d(t_i, t_j)$, which is dominated by the smallest-error pairs. Quantization error is unbiased on average but its $\min$-aggregation over many tokens is biased downward (extreme-value statistics): the rank order is determined by quantization artifacts rather than semantic similarity. Sum or mean aggregation would tolerate quantization; min does not.

\subsection*{F9. PQ-Chamfer for initial retrieval}
\textbf{Hypothesis.} Using PQ-Chamfer as the first-stage ANN retriever (replacing cosine on pooled embeddings) directly captures token-level matches and improves recall@1000.
\textbf{Protocol.} Build inverted index over PQ codes; query is a single pooled embedding (not a token cloud); rank by PQ-Chamfer.
\textbf{Result.} Recall@1000 $-4.2\%$ on NFCorpus.
\textbf{Root cause.} Chamfer is asymmetric and degenerates when one side has a single point: $\mathrm{Chamfer}(\{q\}, D) = \min_{t \in D} d(q,t)$ becomes nearest-neighbor distance to the closest token in $D$, which is dominated by document length and noise tokens. Chamfer's value comes from \emph{cloud-to-cloud} aggregation; single-point queries waste it.

\subsection*{F10. Global graph diffusion}
\textbf{Hypothesis.} Diffusing over the full document graph (rather than local KNN) propagates relevance to multi-hop neighbors that share latent topics.
\textbf{Protocol.} Full graph $G$ with $n^2$ edges weighted by cosine; $T$ steps of $C \leftarrow (1-\alpha) C + \alpha P C$ where $P$ is row-normalized.
\textbf{Result.} NDCG@10 $-7.6\%$ on NFCorpus.
\textbf{Root cause.} The graph spectral gap collapses when too many edges are added: small eigenvalues of $L = I - P$ approach zero and the diffusion mixes toward the stationary distribution (uniform mass on the largest connected component). This is over-smoothing; BFS-style noise dilution dominates the relevance signal after $\sim O(\log n)$ steps.

\subsection*{F11. L3 subspace cross-visit}
\textbf{Hypothesis.} Computing PQ-Chamfer with cross-subspace token matching (token $t_i$ in subspace $s$ matches $t_j$ in subspace $s'$) exploits unequal information content across Qwen3's embedding dimensions.
\textbf{Protocol.} Allow matching across subspace boundaries with permutation-cost penalty; compare to fixed within-subspace matching.
\textbf{Result.} No measurable gain; latency $+3\times$.
\textbf{Root cause.} Local intrinsic dimensionality (LID) analysis shows Qwen3's embedding dimensions carry approximately uniform information content. There is no asymmetry to exploit by cross-subspace matching, so the additional combinatorial freedom only inflates cost.

\subsection*{F12. Binary HDC + popcount}
\textbf{Hypothesis.} Binarizing embeddings (sign of each dimension) and using popcount-based Hamming distance yields $\sim 32\times$ acceleration with bounded accuracy loss.
\textbf{Protocol.} $\mathrm{sign}(x) \in \{-1, +1\}^{4096}$; bitpack and Hamming-rerank.
\textbf{Result.} Effectively no speed-up at the system level.
\textbf{Root cause.} Profiling shows the Chamfer pipeline is bottlenecked by token-cloud I/O and graph construction, not by the inner distance kernel. Optimizing distance computation (which already runs at SIMD throughput) yields negligible end-to-end gain. The hypothesis was diagnostically correct (HDC is faster per op) but operationally irrelevant (op cost is not the bottleneck).

\subsection*{F13. Turing patterns}
\textbf{Hypothesis.} Reaction--diffusion Turing instabilities create stable spatial patterns in score space, yielding self-organized cluster boundaries.
\textbf{Protocol.} Attempted single-species Allen--Cahn with periodic boundary on document graph.
\textbf{Result.} Mathematically impossible to instantiate.
\textbf{Root cause.} Turing instability strictly requires two coupled species (activator + inhibitor) with $D_v / D_u > $ threshold. A single scalar field $C$ cannot exhibit Turing patterns. The hypothesis was a category error.

\subsection*{F14. Tensor diffusion}
\textbf{Hypothesis.} Replacing the scalar diffusion coefficient $D$ with an anisotropic tensor $D_{ij}$ per edge captures direction-dependent relevance flow and recovers F1's failure.
\textbf{Protocol.} Per-edge $D_{ij} \in \mathbb{R}^{4096 \times 4096}$.
\textbf{Result.} Memory infeasible.
\textbf{Root cause.} A $4096 \times 4096$ float32 tensor occupies $\approx 64$~MB; the BEIR FiQA graph has $\sim 10^5$ edges, so tensor diffusion needs $\sim 6.4$~TB of edge state. Even sparsified to 100 nonzeros per edge it is impractical, and the underlying directional signal is still bounded by $O(1/\sqrt{d})$ (F1).

\subsection*{F15. Mixed initial field}
\textbf{Hypothesis.} Initializing the diffusion field with both query-aligned mass and a uniform prior produces more stable smoothing than pure query initialization.
\textbf{Protocol.} $C(x, 0) = (1-\beta) \cdot \mathrm{cos}(q,x) + \beta \cdot 1/n$ for $\beta \in \{0.1, 0.3, 0.5\}$.
\textbf{Result.} Inconsistent: NFCorpus $+3.2\%$, FiQA $-1.5\%$, SCIDOCS $+0.3\%$. No stable improvement across datasets.
\textbf{Root cause.} The optimal $\beta$ depends on the query-prior mass ratio, which varies with dataset (query length, corpus size, baseline cosine concentration). There is no universal $\beta$; it is a per-dataset hyperparameter masquerading as a method, and any apparent gain is hyperparameter overfitting on the development set.

\subsection*{F16. Temperature-scaled advection}
\textbf{Hypothesis.} Scaling $\vec{u}$ by an adaptive ``temperature'' $T(x)$ that softens advection in high-density regions stabilizes F1.
\textbf{Protocol.} $T(x) \propto 1/\mathrm{density}(x)$; $\vec{u}_{\mathrm{eff}} = T(x) \vec{u}$.
\textbf{Result.} Numerical divergence within 10 steps.
\textbf{Root cause.} The graph P\'eclet number $\mathrm{Pe} = \|\vec{u}_{\mathrm{eff}}\| h / D = 0.604$ exceeds the explicit-Euler stability bound $\mathrm{Pe}_{\max} \approx 0.5$. Adaptive scaling violates the CFL condition non-uniformly across the graph, which is harder to fix than baseline advection.

\subsection*{F17. Multi-dimensional alignment}
\textbf{Hypothesis.} Aligning per-document token clouds via Procrustes rotation before Chamfer matching corrects rotational nuisance and improves discrimination.
\textbf{Protocol.} Iterative orthogonal Procrustes with regularization; convergence checked at each step.
\textbf{Result.} 0\% convergence; latency 85.8~ms per document pair.
\textbf{Root cause.} Token clouds in $\mathbb{R}^{4096}$ have no canonical reference frame: a global rotation that aligns one query--document pair misaligns others. Procrustes is well-defined only when an underlying isometry exists; for token clouds it does not, and the iteration cycles indefinitely.

\subsection*{F18. Block-aligned attention}
\textbf{Hypothesis.} Restricting attention to within-block PQ subspaces yields a hybrid of attention and PQ that combines learned and geometric matching.
\textbf{Protocol.} 64-block soft attention; $\mathrm{Pe}_{\mathrm{block}} = 0.226$ analog.
\textbf{Result.} 20\% convergence; unstable on FiQA, SCIDOCS.
\textbf{Root cause.} Block attention reintroduces the directional signal that F1 already proved $O(1/\sqrt{d})$-noisy, but at the block level (subspace dim $= 64$). The block-level signal-to-noise is $1/\sqrt{64} = 0.125$, much improved over the full-space $0.016$, yet the attention mechanism amplifies this still-noisy signal nonlinearly and produces cascading instability.

\subsection*{F19. PCA local projection}
\textbf{Hypothesis.} Per-document PCA to a low-dimensional principal subspace before Chamfer reduces noise and accelerates matching.
\textbf{Protocol.} Local PCA on each document's token cloud, retain top-$k$ components ($k \in \{16, 32, 64\}$).
\textbf{Result.} NDCG@10 within $\pm 0.3\%$ of baseline; latency $+33$~ms per document.
\textbf{Root cause.} Per-document PCA components are not aligned across documents (each PCA basis is data-dependent), so cross-document distances in projected space are not comparable. The marginal accuracy change reflects this incoherence, not a robust signal. Global PCA would align bases but lose per-document structure.

\subsection*{F20. Token-level enhancement on a strong baseline}
\textbf{Hypothesis.} The PQ-Chamfer + Laplacian pipeline improves over any cosine baseline, including strong ones.
\textbf{Protocol.} SciFact, where cosine baseline NDCG@10 $= 0.4483$ (the strongest in our set).
\textbf{Result.} Token-level PQ-Chamfer $-3.1\%$ on SciFact.
\textbf{Root cause.} When the cosine baseline is already near the dataset's empirical ceiling, geometric reranking has little headroom: the residual ranking error is dominated by intrinsic label noise rather than concentration-of-measure noise. PQ-Chamfer's gain scales inversely with baseline quality, as confirmed by the cross-dataset trend in Table~\ref{tab:main}.

\subsection*{F21. Multi-emission coarse filter}
\textbf{Hypothesis.} A first-stage coarse filter that emits multiple candidates per query expands the recall pool and gives the reranker more headroom.
\textbf{Protocol.} Query $\to$ top-$M$ ($M=5$) emissions $\to$ union recall set $\to$ rerank.
\textbf{Result.} Recall@1000 $+22\%$, but NDCG@10 unchanged.
\textbf{Root cause.} The additional recalled documents are marginal (low-similarity), and the reranker's discriminative power on them is at the noise floor: it cannot reliably promote a marginal-but-relevant document past a previously-ranked one. Recall and NDCG@10 measure different things; expanding the candidate pool helps recall but does not necessarily move the top-10.

\subsection*{Synthesis: three families of failure modes}

The 21 falsifications cluster into three mechanisms: (a)~\textbf{curse of orthogonality} (F1, F4, F5, F6, F14, F16, F18) --- any method requiring a directional signal in $\mathbb{R}^{4096}$ is bounded by $O(1/\sqrt{d})$ SNR; (b)~\textbf{aggregation bias} (F7, F8, F9, F10, F19, F21) --- density up-weighting amplifies synonymous redundancy, $\min$-aggregation amplifies tail errors, global graph mixing erases signal, per-document basis incoherence prevents cross-document comparison; (c)~\textbf{numerical and design pathology} (F2, F3, F11, F12, F13, F15, F17, F20) --- score-range dormancy, normalization-induced rescaling, infeasible memory, category errors, hyperparameter overfitting masquerading as method, missing canonical frames, and saturation against strong baselines. The constructive PQ-Chamfer + Laplacian pipeline (Section~\ref{sec:method}) was selected precisely because it avoids all three: it is isotropic (no directional dependence), uses sum-aggregation within subspaces (no min-bias), and operates on local KNN graphs with bounded mixing time.
